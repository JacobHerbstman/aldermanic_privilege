\begin{abstract}
Land use regulation constrains housing supply, but formal zoning rules are only part of the regulatory environment developers face.
I study informal regulatory discretion in Chicago, where ``aldermanic privilege'' allows city council members to block, delay, or reshape development in their wards.
I construct alderman-level stringency scores from permit processing times and compare new construction near ward boundaries, where nearby projects face similar demand conditions but different aldermen.
A one-standard-deviation increase in stringency lowers multifamily floor-area ratio by about 14\% and dwelling units per acre by about 10\%.
Using 2015 ward redistricting as a complementary identification strategy, I also find that assignment toward a ward represented by a one-standard-deviation more stringent incumbent reduces the annual number of high-discretion permits recorded by the city by about 9.5\% from 2015 through 2020.
Listed rents are about 1.7\% higher on the more stringent side of ward boundaries; home sales provide suggestive evidence of prices about 1.2\% higher on that side.
These findings show that informal political discretion can constrain housing supply even when the formal regulatory environment is similar.
\end{abstract}
