\section{Results}
\label{sec:empirical_results}

\subsection{Density at Ward Boundaries}
\label{sec:empirical_results_density}

I first examine whether completed housing is less dense on the more stringent side of the same ward boundary.
I compare parcels that are within 500ft of a boundary segment, so they are exposed to similar neighborhood amenities and demand conditions, but face different aldermen.
I residualize log FAR and log DUPAC using zoning-group, boundary-segment, and construction-year fixed effects plus own-side demographic controls, then estimate side-specific local-linear fits on either side of the boundary.
The estimating equation is:
%
\begin{equation} \label{eq:segment_fe}
    \ln(Y_{iwst}) =
    \tau \text{MoreStringent}_{iwst}
    + \lambda_L D^-_{iwst}
    + \lambda_S D^+_{iwst}
    + X_i'\theta
    + \alpha_{\text{zone group}}
    + \alpha_{\text{segment}}
    + \gamma_t
    + \varepsilon_{iwst},
\end{equation}
%
where $Y_{iwst}$ is FAR or DUPAC for parcel $i$ in ward $w$ on boundary segment $s$, built in year $t$.
$D^-_{iwst}$ and $D^+_{iwst}$ equal distance to the boundary on the more lenient and more stringent sides, respectively, and are zero on the opposite side.
$X_i$ collects own-side demographic controls.
The coefficient $\tau$ is the jump at the boundary from the more lenient side to the more stringent side.

Figure~\ref{fig:rd_fe_plots} shows the boundary discontinuity for all new construction and multifamily new construction.
The estimates are negative in all four panels and largest for multifamily buildings, the projects where aldermanic discretion is most likely to bind.
Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_density} reports shifted-cutoff placebo checks and donut checks around the boundary.

\subsection{Permit Activity After Redistricting}
\label{sec:empirical_results_permits}

The density comparison uses close parcels on opposite sides of a boundary.
As a complementary analysis using within-location variation, I use the 2015 ward redistricting to compare the same census blocks before and after their alderman changes.
Some blocks were reassigned to more stringent aldermen, some were reassigned to more lenient aldermen, and nearby non-switching blocks remained under the same origin ward.
This design holds fixed the physical block and asks whether permit activity changes when the informal regulatory environment shifts.

The outcome is the count of high-discretion permit applications on each census block in each year from 2010 through 2020.
I date permits by application because the outcome of interest is whether a project enters the permitting process, rather than how long it remains under review.
These are permits for projects where aldermen are most likely to matter: new construction, major renovation, demolition, porch construction, and reinstated permits.
I estimate a PPML event study with block fixed effects and border-pair-by-year fixed effects:
%
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:event_study_permits}
\log E[Y_{bt}] = \alpha_b + \lambda_{gt} + \mathbf{X}_b'\gamma_t + \sum_{\tau \neq -1} \beta_\tau \left( \Delta\text{Stringency}_b \times \mathbf{1}[r_{bt} = \tau] \right),
\end{equation}
%
where $Y_{bt}$ is the permit count for block $b$ in year $t$, $\alpha_b$ is a block fixed effect, and $\lambda_{gt}$ is a border-pair-by-year fixed effect.
$r_{bt}$ is the year relative to the 2015 redistricting event.
The vector $\mathbf{X}_b$ contains the block's total pre-redistricting permit volume and an indicator for having no pre-redistricting permits, each allowed to have a different coefficient by year.
The treatment variable $\Delta\text{Stringency}_b$ is the destination alderman's baseline stringency score minus the origin alderman's baseline score, using scores estimated only from permits filed between 2006 and 2014.
Positive values mean the block moved to a more stringent alderman.
I keep only blocks where the 2014 aldermen in both the origin and destination wards remained in office in 2015.
This restriction prevents electoral turnover, which may itself reflect local demand for development, from determining the measured change in stringency.

Figure~\ref{fig:permit_event_study} presents the main permit result.
Before redistricting, blocks that would experience larger increases in aldermanic stringency do not show a differential trend.
After reassignment, permit activity falls.
A one-standard-deviation increase in aldermanic stringency reduces high-discretion permit applications by 13.2\% over the next five years.
The pooled PPML coefficient is $-0.142$ (SE $=0.041$).
This suggests that aldermanic stringency affects not only the density of completed projects but also the flow of projects requiring discretionary review.

\subsection{Suggestive Evidence on Rents and Sale Prices}
\label{sec:empirical_results_prices}

The density and permit results show that more stringent aldermen reduce housing supply on two margins: the intensive margin of completed new construction and the extensive margin of new high-discretion permits that will likely involve the alderman in some capacity.
I next ask whether housing-cost outcomes are higher on the more stringent side of ward boundaries.

For rents and sales, I estimate
%
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:price_rd}
    \log(Y_{ist}) =
    \beta \text{MoreStringent}_{is}
    + X_i'\gamma
    + \alpha_{s t}
    + \varepsilon_{ist},
\end{equation}
%
where $Y_{ist}$ is the real listed rent or real sale price for observation $i$ near boundary segment $s$ in period $t$.
The fixed effects are segment-by-month for rents and segment-by-quarter for sales.
Controls include listing or property characteristics and distances to nearby schools, parks, major roads, CTA stops, and Lake Michigan.
Standard errors are clustered by boundary segment.

Figure~\ref{fig:rental_rd_flat} shows that listed rents are about 3.5\% higher on the more stringent side of a ward boundary after residualizing for segment-by-month fixed effects, hedonic controls, and observed amenity controls.
Figure~\ref{fig:sales_rd_flat} shows a smaller home-sale price gap of about 1.7\%.
Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_price_rd} reports placebo-cutoff checks for both outcomes.
