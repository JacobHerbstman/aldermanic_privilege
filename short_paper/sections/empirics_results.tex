\section{Results}
\label{sec:empirical_results}

\subsection{Density at Ward Boundaries}
\label{sec:empirical_results_density}

I first examine whether completed housing is less dense on the more stringent side of the same ward boundary.
I compare parcels that are within 500ft of a boundary segment, so they are exposed to similar neighborhood amenities and demand conditions, but face different aldermen.
I residualize log FAR and log DUPAC using zoning-group, boundary-segment, and construction-year fixed effects plus own-side demographic controls, then estimate side-specific local-linear fits on either side of the boundary.
The estimating equation is:
%
\begin{equation} \label{eq:segment_fe}
    \ln(Y_{iwst}) =
    \tau \text{MoreStringent}_{iwst}
    + \lambda_L D^-_{iwst}
    + \lambda_S D^+_{iwst}
    + X_i'\theta
    + \alpha_{\text{zone group}}
    + \alpha_{\text{segment}}
    + \gamma_t
    + \varepsilon_{iwst},
\end{equation}
%
where $Y_{iwst}$ is FAR or DUPAC for parcel $i$ in ward $w$ on boundary segment $s$, built in year $t$.
$D^-_{iwst}$ and $D^+_{iwst}$ equal distance to the boundary on the more lenient and more stringent sides, respectively, and are zero on the opposite side.
$X_i$ collects own-side demographic controls.
The coefficient $\tau$ is the jump at the boundary from the more lenient side to the more stringent side.

Table~\ref{tab:density_main_table} reports both versions of the density comparison.
Panel A replaces the side indicator with the standardized aldermanic stringency score, while Panel B reports the binary version of the comparison visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:rd_fe_plots}.
A one-standard-deviation increase in stringency lowers multifamily FAR by about 14\% and multifamily DUPAC by about 10\%.
The binary estimates imply gaps of about 24\% for multifamily FAR and 28\% for multifamily DUPAC.
The all-construction estimates are small and statistically indistinguishable from zero in both specifications.

\subsection{Permit Activity After Redistricting}
\label{sec:empirical_results_permits}

The density comparison uses close parcels on opposite sides of a boundary.
As a complementary analysis using within-location variation, I use the 2015 ward redistricting to compare the same census blocks before and after their ward assignments change.
Some blocks moved toward wards represented before redistricting by more stringent aldermen, some moved toward more lenient aldermen, and nearby non-switching blocks remained under the same origin ward.
This design holds fixed the physical block and asks whether permit activity changes with its assigned regulatory environment.

The outcome is the number of high-discretion permits recorded on each census block in each year from 2010 through 2020.
I count permits by the year their applications began rather than the year they were issued.
The outcome therefore measures when projects observed in the permit data first appeared in the process.
These are the high-discretion permits defined in Section~\ref{subsec:permit_pipeline}.
I estimate a PPML event study with block fixed effects and border-pair-by-year fixed effects:
%
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:event_study_permits}
\log E[Y_{bt}] = \alpha_b + \lambda_{gt} + \mathbf{X}_b'\gamma_t + \sum_{\tau \neq -1} \beta_\tau \left( \Delta\text{Stringency}_b \times \mathbf{1}[r_{bt} = \tau] \right),
\end{equation}
%
where $Y_{bt}$ is the permit count for block $b$ in year $t$, $\alpha_b$ is a block fixed effect, and $\lambda_{gt}$ is a border-pair-by-year fixed effect.
$r_{bt}$ is the year relative to the 2015 redistricting event.
The vector $\mathbf{X}_b$ contains the block's total pre-redistricting permit volume and an indicator for having no pre-redistricting permits, each allowed to have a different coefficient by year.
I measure the change in stringency using the 2014 incumbents in each block's origin and destination wards, with scores estimated only from permits filed between 2006 and 2014.
Positive values mean the block moved to a more stringent alderman, and I keep the measure fixed before the 2015 election.
Some wards elected new aldermen when the map took effect, so the assigned change does not always match the change a block ultimately experienced.
I do not redefine treatment using the 2015 winner because that election may itself reflect local preferences toward development.
I therefore interpret the main estimate as the effect of assigned rather than realized stringency.
Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:permit_event_study_stable} restricts the sample to blocks whose 2014 origin- and destination-ward incumbents both remained in office in 2015.
In that sample, the estimated annual number of high-discretion permits is 12.4\% lower, suggesting that the gap between assigned and realized stringency is not driving the result.

Figure~\ref{fig:permit_event_study} presents the main permit result.
Before redistricting, blocks that would experience larger increases in aldermanic stringency do not show a differential trend.
After reassignment, permit activity falls.
A one-standard-deviation increase in assigned stringency reduces the annual number of high-discretion permits recorded by the city by 9.5\% in the five years following the remap.
The pooled PPML coefficient is $-0.100$ (SE $=0.032$).
This suggests that aldermanic stringency affects not only the density of completed projects but also the flow of projects requiring discretionary review.

\subsection{Suggestive Evidence on Rents and Sale Prices}
\label{sec:empirical_results_prices}

The density and permit results show that more stringent aldermen reduce housing supply on two margins: the intensive margin of completed new construction and the extensive margin of new high-discretion permits that will likely involve the alderman in some capacity.
I next ask whether housing-cost outcomes are higher on the more stringent side of ward boundaries.

For rents and sales, I estimate
%
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:price_rd}
    \log(Y_{ist}) =
    \beta \text{MoreStringent}_{is}
    + X_i'\gamma
    + \alpha_{s t}
    + \varepsilon_{ist},
\end{equation}
%
where $Y_{ist}$ is the real listed rent or real sale price for observation $i$ near boundary segment $s$ in period $t$.
The fixed effects are segment-by-month for rents and segment-by-quarter for sales.
Controls include listing or property characteristics and distances to nearby schools, parks, major roads, CTA stops, and Lake Michigan.
Standard errors are clustered by boundary segment.

Figure~\ref{fig:price_rd_flat}, Panel A, shows that listed rents are about 1.7\% higher on the more stringent side of a ward boundary after residualizing for segment-by-month fixed effects, hedonic controls, and observed amenity controls.
Panel B points to a smaller home-sale price gap of about 1.2\%, although that estimate is statistically imprecise.
Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_price_rd} reports placebo-cutoff checks for both outcomes.
