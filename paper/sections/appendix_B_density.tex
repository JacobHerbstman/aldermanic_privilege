\subsection{Covariate Balance Test}

A key identifying assumption is that neighborhood characteristics vary smoothly across ward boundaries. To test this, I assign each parcel to a census block group via spatial intersection with 2019 ACS geographies and regress demographic variables on the aldermanic strictness score using the same fixed effects specification as the main regressions (zoning-group, segment, and year fixed effects), restricting to parcels within 500 feet of the border. Results (not shown) indicate null effects across racial composition, median income, education, homeownership, median rent, home values, and population density, supporting the interpretation that density differences at ward borders are driven by aldermanic behavior rather than sorting on observables.\footnote{Balance test table to be added in a future draft.}

\subsection{Alternative Fixed Effects: Ward-Pair}

Table~\ref{tab:fe_pair_year} replaces segment fixed effects with ward-pair fixed effects, which pool across all segments of a given ward pair. This is a coarser comparison---ward-pair borders can stretch for miles through different neighborhoods---but it uses more variation. The results are similar in sign and magnitude to the main segment FE estimates in Table~\ref{tab:segment_fe_500}.

\subsection{Donut RD Robustness}

One concern with spatial RD designs is that observations precisely at the boundary may be unusual---for example, if ward boundaries follow major streets that independently affect development. Figure~\ref{fig:rd_donut} implements a donut specification \citep{barreca_saving_2011, barreca_heapinginduced_2016} that excludes parcels within 25 or 50 feet of the boundary. The discontinuity persists, suggesting the results are not driven by idiosyncratic features of parcels at the cutoff.
