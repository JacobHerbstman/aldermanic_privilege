\section{Event Study: Permits}
\label{sec:empirical_results_permits}

\subsection{Redistricting Design}

While the regression discontinuity design compares parcels within a few hundred feet of each other, it is possible that these areas are not unobservably identical. 
To complement my main regression discontinuity approach, I test whether permit changes when a fixed location is reassigned to a different alderman.
I exploit the 2015 ward redistricting as a natural experiment, which reassigned census blocks to new aldermen for reasons unrelated to block-level development activity.
This lets me compare the same census block before and after its alderman changes, so block fixed effects absorb any time-invariant differences across locations.

Ward boundaries in Chicago are redrawn following each decennial census to equalize populations across wards.
The 2015 redistricting (based on the 2010 Census) reassigned many census blocks to new aldermen while leaving nearby blocks under the same representation.
This creates a difference-in-differences comparison between blocks that switched aldermen and nearby blocks that stayed in the same ward.
The treatment is bidirectional, as some blocks moved to more stringent aldermen and some to more lenient ones.
In the main specification, treatment enters continuously as the destination alderman's score minus the origin alderman's score, so positive values mean a move to a more stringent alderman and negative values mean a move to a more lenient one.
The identifying assumption is that, absent the change in aldermanic representation, treated and control blocks would have followed parallel trends in permit activity.

Several features of the design support this assumption.
Redistricting is driven primarily by population equalization and political bargaining over ward composition, rather than by parcel-level development activity on specific blocks.
To tighten the counterfactual, I restrict the control group to blocks that remained in the \textit{origin} ward, so treated and control blocks shared the same pre-treatment regulatory environment.
I also drop non-switching blocks in origin wards where the alderman changed between 2014 and 2015 to avoid potentially endogenous aldermanic turnover through the ward electing someone which could be partially driven by housing policy positions of the candidates.

Figure~\ref{fig:treatment_control_maps} shows the citywide distribution of treated and control blocks for the 2015 redistricting.

\subsection{Estimating Equation}

The outcome is the count of high-discretion permits issued on each census block in each year from 2010 through 2020.
These are permits for projects where aldermen are most likely to matter: new construction, renovation or alteration, demolition, porch construction, and reinstated permits.
Because permits are counts that aggregate naturally to a balanced block-year panel, with every block observed in every year (including zeros), I can include block fixed effects and identify entirely off within-block variation over time.
I estimate the event study using Poisson pseudo-maximum likelihood (PPML), with the log conditional mean given by:
%
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:event_study_permits}
\log E[Y_{bt}] = \alpha_b + \lambda_{gt} + \sum_{\tau \neq -1} \beta_\tau \left( \Delta\text{Stringency}_b \times \mathbf{1}[t = \tau] \right)
\end{equation}
%
where $Y_{bt}$ is the count of high-discretion permits issued on block $b$ in year $t$, $\alpha_b$ is a block fixed effect, and $\lambda_{gt}$ is a border-pair-by-year fixed effect.
In the 2015-cohort design, the treatment variable $\Delta\text{Stringency}_b$ is the destination alderman's baseline stringency score minus the origin alderman's baseline stringency score, where origin and destination aldermen are assigned using the 2014 pre-redistricting ward-to-alderman mapping.

The block fixed effects absorb all time-invariant characteristics of each block, so identification comes from within-block changes over time: the same physical block before and after its alderman changes.
The border-pair-by-year fixed effects absorb local shocks that affect all blocks near a given ward boundary in a given year, so the comparison is between blocks near the same boundary that experienced different changes in aldermanic representation.
The boundary neighborhoods are defined using the cohort's pre-redistricting ward map: switched blocks use the fixed origin-destination boundary created by redistricting, and non-switched controls use their nearest incident pre-redistricting boundary.
The sample uses the 2015 redistricting cohort from 2010 through 2020 and keeps all census blocks with a median permit within 1000ft of a ward boundary for additional power.
Standard errors are clustered at the census block level.

\subsection{Results}

Figure~\ref{fig:permit_event_study} presents the main result.
In the first full year following the redistricting event, blocks that moved to a one standard deviation more stringent alderman see a large and sudden drop in high-discretion permitting activity, which rebounds slightly but persists at a lower 
level than in the pre-period.

Figure~\ref{fig:permit_event_study_split} traces the effect separately for blocks that moved to more stringent versus more lenient aldermen.
The plotted coefficients are directionally consistent with the baseline specification: blocks assigned to more stringent aldermen tend to show lower permit issuance, while blocks assigned to more lenient aldermen tend to move in the opposite direction.
So the result is not driven by only one direction of aldermen turnover, but rather by both lenient and stringent aldermen changing the permitting activity in their wards.

Table~\ref{tab:did_permit_2015_current} collapses the post-period into a single pooled differences-in-differences (DiD) coefficient.
The pooled PPML estimate is $-0.077$ (SE $= 0.037$), which is about a 7.4\% decline for a one standard deviation increase in alderman stringency.
This is a direct measure of how aldermanic discretion affects project flow: more stringent aldermen do not simply alter the scale of completed buildings, they reduce the number of projects that require their engagement.

As a placebo, I run the same specification on lower-discretion permits: routine permit types outside the high-discretion group that are less likely to attract community attention or demand aldermanic intervention.
Figure~\ref{fig:permit_event_study_low_disc} shows the corresponding event study.
The plot has a very small drop in permitting but is much smoother through the redistricting event, and the individual years and the pooled post-period estimate are not statistically significant.
This makes it less likely that the main result is picking up a general shift in permitting activity or bureaucratic capacity of specific aldermen unrelated to the housing policy preferences of the alderman.

\subsection{Interpretation and Additional Checks}

Because the specification uses block fixed effects, the design is less exposed to time-invariant differences across blocks that could bias the border discontinuity estimates.

The permits studied here are also not simply inputs to the stringency index.
The stringency score is constructed from processing \textit{times} on all high-discretion permits across a ward, while the event study outcome is the \textit{count} of high-discretion permits issued on individual blocks.
Given that permit processing times and permit counts are only weakly correlated at the ward-month level (see Appendix Table~\ref{tab:permit_volume_correlation}), I am confident this result is not due to a mechanical correlation between the two variables.
