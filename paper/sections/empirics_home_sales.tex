\section{Downstream Price Evidence: Home Sales}
\label{sec:empirical_results_home_sales}

\subsection{Design}

If the supply restrictions documented in the previous two sections are economically meaningful, they should eventually be capitalized into housing prices. To test this, I apply the same redistricting design from Section~\ref{sec:empirical_results_permits} to home sale prices. I treat the price evidence as suggestive rather than definitive: the results are directionally consistent with the supply story, but they are noisier than the permit results and harder to separate from demand-side channels.

The specification is estimated at the transaction level:
%
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:event_study_sales}
\ln(\text{Price}_{it}) = \alpha_{gs} + \lambda_{gt} + X_{it}'\gamma + \sum_{\tau \neq -1} \beta_\tau \left( \Delta\text{Stringency}_i \times \mathbf{1}[t = \tau] \right) + \varepsilon_{it}
\end{equation}
%
where $\ln(\text{Price}_{it})$ is the log sale price for transaction $i$ at event time $t$, $\alpha_{gs}$ is a border-pair-by-origin-side fixed effect, $\lambda_{gt}$ is a border-pair-by-year fixed effect, and $X_{it}$ includes hedonic controls for building square footage, land square footage, building age, bedrooms, bathrooms, and garage presence. The sample is restricted to transactions within 1,000 feet of ward boundaries with uniform weights. Standard errors are clustered at the census block level. I focus on the 2015 redistricting cohort because the 2023 redistricting provides too little post-period data for meaningful inference. The sample window runs from 2010 to 2020, providing roughly five years on each side.

\subsection{Results}

Figure~\ref{fig:event_study_sales} presents the event study. Panel A shows the continuous treatment specification. The pre-period coefficients show some variation but are generally centered around zero. Following redistricting, home prices on blocks assigned to more stringent aldermen rise gradually relative to the reference period. The trajectory is consistent with a supply restriction channel operating through reduced new construction: the price response builds slowly as the housing market tightens, rather than jumping immediately at redistricting.

Panel B shows the continuous split, allowing separate slopes for blocks that experienced positive versus negative changes in stringency. The pattern is directionally consistent with theory: positive price effects for blocks moving to more stringent aldermen, slight declines for blocks moving to more lenient ones. But the confidence intervals are wide, so the split results remain suggestive rather than definitive.

\subsection{Interpretation}

The home price evidence is consistent with the supply story but should not be asked to carry more weight than it can. The gradual post-redistricting trajectory matches the prediction from the time-to-build framework, where prices respond slowly as reduced permitting translates into a tighter housing stock, and the direction of the effect aligns with both the density and permit results.

But the estimates are imprecise, the sample is smaller than for permits, and I cannot fully rule out that part of the price response reflects demand rather than supply. A more stringent alderman might raise prices not only by restricting supply but also by signaling neighborhood stability or desirability to prospective buyers. Future work incorporating rental outcomes, where the supply channel should be more direct, may help sharpen the price evidence. For now, the home sales results are best read as one more piece in a body of evidence whose strongest elements are the density discontinuity and the permit event study.
