\section{Event Study: Home Sale Prices}
\label{sec:empirical_results_home_sales}

\subsection{Design}

If the supply restrictions documented in the previous two sections are economically meaningful, they should eventually be capitalized into housing prices. To test this, I apply the same redistricting variation from Section~\ref{sec:empirical_results_permits} to home sale prices. I treat the price evidence as suggestive rather than definitive: the results are directionally consistent with the supply story, but they are noisier than the permit results and harder to separate from demand-side channels.

The specification is estimated at the transaction level:
%
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:event_study_sales}
\ln(\text{Price}_{iy}) = \alpha_{gs} + \lambda_{gy} + X_{iy}'\gamma + \sum_{\tau \neq -1} \beta_\tau \left( \Delta\text{Stringency}_i \times \mathbf{1}[\text{event time} = \tau] \right) + \varepsilon_{iy}
\end{equation}
%
where $\ln(\text{Price}_{iy})$ is the log sale price for transaction $i$ in sale year $y$, $\alpha_{gs}$ is a border-pair-by-origin-side fixed effect, $\lambda_{gy}$ is a border-pair-by-year fixed effect, and $X_{iy}$ includes hedonic controls for building square footage, land square footage, building age, bedrooms, bathrooms, and garage presence. The sample is restricted to transactions within 1,000 feet of ward boundaries with uniform weights. Standard errors are clustered at the census block level. I focus on the 2015 redistricting cohort because the 2023 redistricting provides too little post-period data for meaningful inference. The sample window runs from 2010 to 2020, providing roughly five years on each side.

The fixed effects structure here differs from the permit specification in Section~\ref{sec:empirical_results_permits}, and the reason is the nature of the outcome data. Permits are counts that aggregate to a balanced block-year panel, making block fixed effects natural: every block is observed in every year, even if the count is zero. Home sales, by contrast, are individual transactions with property-level hedonic characteristics (square footage, age, lot size) that vary across properties within the same block and that matter for price. Many blocks have zero or one sale in a given year, so collapsing to block-year level would discard the hedonic variation that helps isolate the price effect from compositional changes. I therefore use border-pair-by-side fixed effects, which group blocks by their pre-redistricting ward and boundary location, combined with border-pair-by-year fixed effects that absorb local time-varying shocks. This means identification comes from comparing treated and control blocks on the same side of the same boundary in the same year, rather than from within-block variation over time as in the permit specification. The parallel trends assumption is correspondingly stronger: treated and control blocks must be on similar price trajectories absent redistricting. The flat pre-trends in the event study below support this assumption, and the hedonic controls help absorb compositional differences across blocks.

\subsection{Results}

Figure~\ref{fig:event_study_sales} presents the event study. Panel A shows the continuous treatment specification. The pre-period coefficients show some variation but are generally centered around zero. Following redistricting, home prices on blocks assigned to more stringent aldermen rise gradually relative to the reference period. The trajectory is consistent with a supply restriction channel operating through reduced new construction: the price response builds slowly as the housing market tightens, rather than jumping immediately at redistricting.

Panel B shows the continuous split, allowing separate slopes for blocks that experienced positive versus negative changes in stringency. The pattern is directionally consistent with theory: positive price effects for blocks moving to more stringent aldermen, slight declines for blocks moving to more lenient ones. But the confidence intervals are wide, so the split results remain suggestive rather than definitive.

\subsection{Interpretation}

The home price evidence is consistent with the supply story, but also with future expectations about housing supply in that ward. The gradual post-redistricting trajectory matches the prediction from the time-to-build framework, where prices respond slowly as reduced permitting translates into a tighter housing stock, and the direction of the effect aligns with both the density and permit results.

However, it is unclear if these price increases reflect the supply-side effects or other factors. For example, a more stringent alderman could signal less intense development in the future, which could raise home prices as they capitalize those expectations.
