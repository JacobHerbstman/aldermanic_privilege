\section{Event Study: Home Sale Prices}
\label{sec:empirical_results_home_sales}

\subsection{Design}

If the supply restrictions documented in the previous two sections are economically meaningful, they should eventually be capitalized into housing prices. To test that idea, I apply the same redistricting variation from Section~\ref{sec:empirical_results_permits} to home sale prices.

The specification is estimated at the transaction level:
%
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:event_study_sales}
\ln(\text{Price}_{iy}) = \alpha_{gs} + \lambda_{gy} + X_{iy}'\gamma + \sum_{\tau \neq -1} \beta_\tau \left( \Delta\text{Stringency}_i \times \mathbf{1}[\text{event time} = \tau] \right) + \varepsilon_{iy}
\end{equation}
%
where $\ln(\text{Price}_{iy})$ is the log sale price for transaction $i$ in sale year $y$, $\alpha_{gs}$ is a border-pair-by-origin-side fixed effect, $\lambda_{gy}$ is a border-pair-by-year fixed effect, and $X_{iy}$ includes hedonic controls for building square footage, land square footage, building age, bedrooms, bathrooms, and garage presence. The sample is restricted to transactions within 1,000 feet of ward boundaries with uniform weights. Standard errors are clustered at the census block level. I focus on the 2015 redistricting cohort because the 2023 redistricting provides too little post-period data for meaningful inference. The sample window runs from 2010 to 2020, providing roughly five years on each side.

The fixed-effects structure here differs from the permit specification in Section~\ref{sec:empirical_results_permits}, and the reason lies in the outcome data. Permits are counts that aggregate naturally to a balanced block-year panel: every block is observed in every year, even if the count is zero. Home sales, by contrast, are individual transactions with property-level hedonic characteristics (square footage, age, lot size) that vary across properties within the same block and matter for price. Many blocks have zero or one sale in a given year, so collapsing to block-year level would throw away the hedonic variation that helps isolate the price effect from compositional changes. I therefore use border-pair-by-side fixed effects, which group blocks by their pre-redistricting ward and boundary location, together with border-pair-by-year fixed effects that absorb local time-varying shocks. Identification thus comes from comparing treated and control blocks on the same side of the same boundary in the same year, rather than from within-block variation over time as in the permit specification. The parallel-trends assumption is correspondingly stronger: treated and control blocks must be on similar price trajectories absent redistricting. The flat pre-trends in the event study below support this assumption, and the hedonic controls help absorb compositional differences across blocks.

\subsection{Results}

Figure~\ref{fig:event_study_sales} presents the event study. Panel A shows the continuous treatment specification. The pre-period coefficients move around somewhat but are generally centered near zero. Following redistricting, home prices on blocks assigned to more stringent aldermen rise gradually relative to the reference period. The trajectory fits a supply-restriction channel operating through reduced new construction: the price response builds slowly as the housing market tightens rather than jumping immediately at redistricting.

Panel B shows the continuous split, allowing separate slopes for blocks that experienced positive versus negative changes in stringency. The pattern is directionally consistent with theory: price effects are positive for blocks moving to more stringent aldermen and slightly negative for blocks moving to more lenient ones. But the confidence intervals are wide, so the split results remain suggestive rather than definitive.

\subsection{Interpretation}

The price increase could operate through multiple channels, and I do not try to isolate a single one. The most direct mechanism is the supply channel documented above: more stringent aldermen reduce the flow of permits and the density of new construction, which tightens the local housing stock and raises prices. But prices could also respond through an expectations channel, in which a more stringent alderman signals less future development, something incumbent homeowners may value. Both channels fit the rest of the paper's evidence, and both imply a transfer from prospective buyers to incumbent owners.

The gradual post-redistricting trajectory is consistent with either story. Supply restrictions take time to bind as the existing housing stock turns over slowly, and expectations about future neighborhood character would also be capitalized gradually as buyers and sellers learn about the new alderman's stance. Even without separating those channels, the price result adds an important piece to the paper: aldermanic stringency moves housing costs in the predicted direction. Stringency reduces density at ward boundaries (Section~\ref{sec:empirical_results_density}), reduces the flow of new permits when the alderman changes (Section~\ref{sec:empirical_results_permits}), and is associated with modestly higher prices---linking regulatory discretion to housing market outcomes.
