\section{Institutional Background and Data}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Aldermanic Privilege and the Development Process}

Chicago's City Council consists of 50 aldermen, each representing a single ward.
Unlike most cities, where the mayor or a planning commission controls land use
decisions, Chicago's informal norms give each alderman broad authority over
development within ward boundaries.
This practice, known as ``aldermanic
privilege'' or ``aldermanic prerogative,'' rests on custom rather than formal law, in part because Chicago has no city charter.
It operates through a norm of deference: on matters affecting a
single ward, other aldermen defer to the local representative, and city agencies
follow the alderman's lead \citep{thale_aldermanic_2005}.
The convention is strong
enough that aldermen have often been described as ``little mayors'' governing
their wards as personal fiefdoms \citep{einhorn_property_1991}.
One alderman
described the system candidly: ``I often liken the City of Chicago to a feudal
system, where the mayor is sort of a de facto king.
And each alderman is the lord
of their individual fiefdom.''
\citep{keefe_pregnancy_2013}

\paragraph{The formal mechanism.}
Before Mayor Lightfoot eliminated the
practice in July 2019, aldermanic authority over permitting had a formal
institutional expression.
The Department of Buildings required an ``aldermanic
acknowledgement letter'' for building permits above a threshold value, giving the
local alderman a formal 10-day hold on permit issuance---embedding aldermanic review
directly into permit issuance as a matter of city procedure, not simply
informal custom \citep{city_of_chicago_sixty-day_2019}.
Ald.\ Carlos Ramirez-Rosa
described the old norm: ``Rather than an alderman just picking up the phone and
saying, `stop that permit,' we are going to make it transparent.''
Ald.\ James Cappleman was blunter: ``Aldermen right now,
we have 50 czars.''
\citep{schutz_lightfoot_2019}

Mayor Lightfoot's 2019 executive order eliminated the formal hold
\citep{mayors_press_office_mayor_2019}, but the 60-day review her administration
conducted that summer documented how deeply prerogative had penetrated agency
practice: department by department, city agencies had organized their workflows
around aldermanic deference, and those informal norms proved far more durable than
the formal rule \citep{city_of_chicago_sixty-day_2019}.
As Lightfoot herself put it: ``If
you've got 50 separate fiefdoms that you have to deal with on issues like affordable
housing, or just getting a sign on your building, it makes doing business with the
city of Chicago damn near impossible.''
\citep{spielman_lightfoot_2019} The
Metropolitan Planning Council reached the same conclusion from the developer side,
finding that ``before a development even gets to the point of being indefinitely
delayed, many never make it past `Go' because of required aldermanic letters of
support.''
\citep{metropolitan_planning_council_unfettered_2019}

\paragraph{How prerogative shapes development.}
Aldermanic
discretion enters well before formal permit issuance and continues after a project is filed.

\textit{Pre-application.}
Experienced Chicago developers know that any project
requiring rezoning, or smaller project modifications, will require 
obtaining the alderman's support before formal papers are filed.
This is not a
legal requirement, but it is an operational reality: without the alderman's support, a
developer risks investing heavily in design, legal, and engineering work
before facing rejection at the political stage.
One anonymous Chicago architect
described the dynamic: ``To get the alderman, you have to get the neighborhood
group, and that's where the trouble comes in.
It's fraught with risk, even to have
this conversation.''
\citep{giangreco_game_2024}

\textit{Rezoning.}
For projects requiring a change to the zoning code, the
alderman's role is effectively dispositive.
Although zoning amendments formally
require a City Council vote, the council ``routinely shows unquestioning deference
to local aldermen even in the absence of any articulated local concern''
\citep{chase_chicago_2023}.
Equally consequential is the alderman's ability to
preemptively \textit{downzone} parcels to foreclose future development.
Joseph
Schwieterman, a DePaul University professor who studies Chicago land use, noted
that the practice ``creates real uncertainty for real estate developers when
downzoning becomes ad hoc or driven by the desire of an alderman to settle scores.''
\citep{gallun_ad_2019} Documented episodes include Ald.\ Brendan Reilly's
proposals to downzone a River North site where a developer had proposed a 60-story
tower and a separate site to block a 615-room hotel, and a Pilsen downzoning
orchestrated by Ald.\ Solis that stripped a parcel of its residential designation
to kill a planned 465-unit apartment project.

\textit{Permit processing.}
For projects that proceed by right under existing
zoning, the alderman still retains substantial informal influence over the Department of
Buildings permit queue.
The corruption case against Ald.\ Ed Burke illustrated the
mechanism in an extreme form: federal prosecutors documented how his staff
withheld routine permits---including a driveway permit for a fast-food
restaurant---until the property owner directed legal business to Burke's law firm
\citep{spielman_aldermen_2019}.
The case made visible what developers had long
understood: in Chicago, permit timing is another lever aldermen can use to delay and suppress
projects they oppose.

\paragraph{A recent example.}
Developer Fern Hill began community
outreach in 2021, proposing a roughly 500-unit mixed-income tower at a
transit-accessible site in the 2nd Ward, with approximately 100 affordable units
and an estimated \$2.5 million in annual property tax revenue.
After four years and
more than ten community meetings, Ald.\ Brian Hopkins announced in January 2025
that he would not advance the project, citing opposition from residents of
neighboring high-rises.
He was explicit about the source of his authority:
``Ultimately I am the decision maker on this.
I'm not going to hide from that.
This is a zoning decision and in Chicago we have something called aldermanic
prerogative.''
\citep{achong_shortened_2025} Within two weeks the developer
resubmitted a scaled-down proposal---fewer floors, fewer units---and Hopkins
approved it.
The episode captures both margins this paper studies: the approved
project is smaller than what the developer proposed (intensive margin), and the
four-year ordeal signals to other developers the cost of attempting large projects
in wards with active aldermanic gatekeeping (extensive margin).

\paragraph{Federal scrutiny.}
The consequences of this institution have
attracted federal attention.
In October 2023, the U.S.\ Department of Housing and
Urban Development issued findings from a five-year investigation stemming from a
2018 complaint by the Chicago Area Fair Housing Alliance and nine other advocacy
groups.
HUD concluded that aldermanic prerogative functions as an illegal ``local
veto'' over affordable housing proposals: ``the city affords each of its 50 wards a
local veto over proposals to build affordable housing and that many majority-white
wards use the local veto to block, deter or downsize such proposals''
\citep{chase_chicago_2023}. 
The practice has also been accused of accentuating racial segregation and violating civil rights law by outside groups \citep{shriver_center_on_poverty_law_city_2019}.
 The question of whether to reform or abolish aldermanic privilege remains a central issue in Chicago politics.\footnote{See
\url{https://news.wttw.com/elections/voters-guide/2023/races/city-council}, where
each city council candidate was asked specifically about their stance on aldermanic
prerogative.}

\subsection{Building Permits and the Permit Pipeline}

The building permits data come from the City of Chicago's open data portal and include all permit applications filed since 2006 with valid geographic coordinates and non-negative processing times.
I divide permits into two groups based on how much aldermanic discretion they are likely to face.

\textit{High-discretion permits} are permits for projects where aldermen are most likely to matter: new construction, major renovation, demolition, porch construction, and reinstated permits.\footnote{Porch construction permits have received extra scrutiny since the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2003_Chicago_balcony_collapse}{2003 balcony collapse}.}
These are the permit types most likely to draw community opposition and face delays due to community meetings or direct aldermanic intervention.
\textit{Low-discretion permits} are all remaining permit types outside this high-discretion group, excluding signs.
I use them as a placebo comparison group because they are generally less exposed to discretionary aldermanic intervention.

This distinction helps separate aldermanic discretion from general permitting capacity.
High-discretion permits are the ones most likely to require interaction with the alderman.
Low-discretion permits provide a useful comparison group: if the stringency scores are picking up something specific to the discretionary layer rather than general bureaucratic capacity, the effects should be concentrated in high-discretion permits and largely absent for low-discretion ones.

The descriptive patterns are consistent with this interpretation.
High-discretion permits take much longer to process and show more cross-alderman dispersion in mean processing time.
The average high-discretion permit takes 57.3 days from application to issuance, compared with 26.2 days for lower-discretion permits; the medians are 30 days and 6 days.
Across ward-month cells, longer mean processing times for high-discretion permits are associated with \textit{lower} permit volume, while low-discretion processing times are weakly positively correlated with permit volume, suggesting that they reflect administrative capacity rather than regulatory friction.
Figure~\ref{fig:permit_high_low_processing} shows these patterns.
Appendix Table~\ref{tab:permit_summary_stats} reports the corresponding summary statistics, and Appendix Table~\ref{tab:permit_volume_correlation} reports the relationship between processing time and ward-month permit volume.

\subsection{Aldermanic Stringency Scores}
\label{subsec:stringency}

I construct the stringency score using permit applications through December 2022 by residualizing permit processing times against ward and permit characteristics, then extracting the alderman-specific component.
The first stage regresses log processing time at the permit level on ward-level demographic controls (median household income, racial composition, homeownership rate, population), permit-location geographic controls (distance to CBD, lakefront proximity, CTA rail access), lagged ward-month permit volume, and fixed effects for year-month, permit type, and review type:
%
\begin{equation} \label{eq:stringency_reg1}
    \log(\text{Processing Time}_{i}) = \mathbf{X}_i \beta + \gamma_{\text{ym}} + \delta_{\text{pt}} + \zeta_{\text{rt}} + \epsilon_{i}
\end{equation}
%
where $\gamma_{\text{ym}}$ are year-month fixed effects, $\delta_{\text{pt}}$ are permit-type fixed effects, and $\zeta_{\text{rt}}$ are review-type fixed effects.
Table~\ref{tab:stage1_residualization} in the Appendix reports the coefficients on the stage-one controls.

The second stage aggregates these residuals to the ward-month level and projects the ward-month mean residual onto alderman indicator variables and the same lagged ward-month permit volume control, weighted by the number of permits observed in each ward-month:
%
\begin{equation} \label{eq:stringency_reg2}
    \bar{\hat{\epsilon}}_{wm} = \mu + \alpha_{a(w,m)} + \lambda \text{Volume}_{w,m-1} + \eta_{wm}
\end{equation}
%
Here $a(w,m)$ denotes the alderman serving ward $w$ in month $m$.
The raw alderman fixed effects $\hat{\alpha}_a$ can be noisy for aldermen with few permits.
I therefore apply empirical Bayes shrinkage \citep{kline_systemic_2022, kline_discrimination_2024} to pull imprecise estimates toward the grand mean, then standardize the final scores to have mean zero and unit standard deviation.

Appendix~\ref{app:stringency_validation} reports two validation checks.
Appendix Table~\ref{tab:permit_validation} shows that higher stringency predicts fewer high-discretion permits and more evidence of regulatory friction in ward-month data.
Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:predecessor_successor_stringency} shows that the correlation between predecessor and successor aldermen within the same ward is relatively low ($\rho \approx 0.18$), supporting the interpretation that the index captures an alderman-specific component of the development process rather than a fixed ward trait.

Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:aldermanic_stringency_scores} shows the distribution of the final stringency scores, and Figure~\ref{fig:alderman_stringency_map} maps them across the city.
The scores vary in wealthy and poor neighborhoods, on the North Side and South Side, which helps rule out the idea that they are simply proxying for location or local demand conditions.
The score also appears specific to discretionary development friction rather than general permitting slowness.
Appendix Table~\ref{tab:low_discretion_placebo} shows that the main stringency index does not predict residualized processing times for low-discretion permits, and Appendix Table~\ref{tab:low_discretion_density_placebo} shows that a placebo score built from low-discretion permits does not predict density outcomes at ward boundaries.

\subsection{New Construction Data}

I construct a dataset of all new residential construction in Chicago between 2006 and 2022 using publicly available data from the Cook County Assessor's Office.
For buildings with up to six units, I use the residential improvement characteristics data, which provide building-level information on dwelling units, stories, and building area.
For larger multifamily buildings, I use the commercial valuation dataset, which covers properties assessed under multifamily model groups and reports unit counts, building area, and land area.
I merge both sources with the Assessor's parcel universe for precise location coordinates, assign each parcel to its ward using City of Chicago shapefiles, and define the construction date as June 15 of the recorded year built.
I keep parcels with positive lot area, positive building area, and at least one dwelling unit.

For each parcel I compute two standard density measures: Floor Area Ratio (FAR), defined as total building area divided by lot area, and Dwelling Units Per Acre (DUPAC), defined as dwelling units divided by lot acreage.
Distance to the nearest ward boundary is computed after projecting parcels and boundaries to the Illinois East State Plane coordinate system and is reported in feet.

Table~\ref{tab:summary_stats} presents summary statistics.
The full sample contains 12,476 new residential buildings.
The 2,132 multifamily buildings (2+ units) are reported separately because aldermanic discretion is likely to matter most for larger projects.
As a result, I focus on the multifamily sample but show that a gap exists for all new construction as well, albeit smaller in magnitude.

\subsection{Rental Listings}

The rental data come from RentHub, a company that scrapes rental listings nationwide.
The data start in 2014 and I use listings through December 2022 to match my other dataset, and restrict to listings within the Chicago city limits.
The raw data are scrape-level rows, so the same listing can appear many times in the same month.
I clean addresses, coordinates, rent, bedrooms, bathrooms, square footage, and building type, then build property and floorplan proxies from normalized address, rounded coordinates, beds, baths, square feet, and building type.
The main rental panel is one observation per floorplan proxy per month, with rent equal to the median of the daily median asking rent observed during that month.
Before estimating price regressions, I deflate listed rents to 2022 dollars using the monthly Chicago CPI-U all-items series.
As a benchmark, Appendix Table~\ref{tab:rental_benchmark_growth} compares the cleaned listing panel to Zillow’s Chicago Observed Rent Index and finds broadly similar real citywide rent levels over the sample period,
and Appendix~\ref{app:data_sources} gives additional detail on the data cleaning process.

\subsection{Home Sales}

Home sales data come from the Cook County Assessor's Office and cover residential transactions from 1999 to the present.
The price analysis uses sales from 2006 through 2022 to match the paper's main outcome window.
I restrict the sample to arm's-length transactions of single-family homes and small multifamily buildings (2-6 units), excluding nominal transfers, land-only sales, and intrafamily conveyances.
Sale prices are first deflated to 2022 dollars using the monthly Chicago CPI-U all-items series and then winsorized at the 1st and 99th percentiles.
Each transaction is geocoded by its full 14-digit PIN and assigned to the relevant ward based on the boundaries in effect at the sale date.
Property-level hedonic controls such as building area, land area, building age, bedrooms, bathrooms, and garage come from the same-tax-year assessor record.

For both rents and sales, the price analysis uses parcel or listing coordinates projected from longitude-latitude to EPSG:3435 before any distance calculation.
The amenity controls are distances to the nearest school, Chicago Park District park-boundary polygon, major street, distance to the nearest CTA stop, and distance to Lake Michigan.

As with the rental data, more detail on the data construction process can be found in Appendix~\ref{app:data_sources}.
