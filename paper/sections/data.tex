\section{Institutional Background and Data}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Aldermanic Privilege and the Development Process}

Chicago's City Council consists of 50 aldermen, each representing a single ward.
Unlike most cities, where the mayor or a planning commission controls land use
decisions, Chicago's informal norms give each alderman near-total authority over
development within their ward boundaries. This practice, known as ``aldermanic
privilege'' or ``aldermanic prerogative,'' has no basis in the city charter or
municipal code. It operates through a simple convention: on matters affecting a
single ward, other aldermen defer to the local representative, and city agencies
follow the alderman's lead \citep{thale_aldermanic_2005}. The convention is strong
enough that aldermen have historically been described as ``little mayors'' governing
their wards as personal fiefdoms \citep{einhorn_property_1991}. One alderman
described the system candidly: ``I often liken the City of Chicago to a feudal
system, where the mayor is sort of a de facto king. And each alderman is the lord
of their individual fiefdom.'' \citep{keefe_pregnancy_2013}

\paragraph{The formal mechanism.} Before Mayor Lightfoot eliminated the
practice in July 2019, aldermanic authority over permitting had a formal
institutional expression. The Department of Buildings required an ``aldermanic
acknowledgement letter'' for building permits above a threshold value, giving the
local alderman a formal 10-day hold on permit issuance---embedding aldermanic review
directly into the permitting pipeline as a matter of city procedure, not merely
informal custom \citep{noauthor_city_2019}. Ald.\ Carlos Ramirez-Rosa
described the old norm: ``Rather than an alderman just picking up the phone and
saying, `stop that permit,' we are going to make it transparent.''
\citep{vinicky_lightfoot_2019} Ald.\ James Cappleman was blunter: ``Aldermen right now,
we have 50 czars.'' \citep{vinicky_lightfoot_2019}

Mayor Lightfoot's 2019 executive order eliminated the formal hold
\citep{mayors_press_office_mayor_2019}, but the 60-day review her administration
conducted that summer documented how deeply prerogative had penetrated agency
practice: department by department, city agencies had organized their workflows
around aldermanic deference, and those informal norms proved far more durable than
the formal rule \citep{noauthor_city_2019}. As Lightfoot herself put it: ``If
you've got 50 separate fiefdoms that you have to deal with on issues like affordable
housing, or just getting a sign on your building, it makes doing business with the
city of Chicago damn near impossible.'' \citep{vinicky_lightfoot_2019} The
Metropolitan Planning Council reached the same conclusion from the developer side,
finding that ``before a development even gets to the point of being indefinitely
delayed, many never make it past `Go' because of required aldermanic letters of
support.'' \citep{noauthor_unfettered_nodate}

\paragraph{How prerogative shapes the development pipeline.} Aldermanic
discretion operates at every stage of the development process, not only at the
formal permitting step.

\textit{Pre-application.} Experienced Chicago developers know that any project
requiring rezoning---or even a discretionary sign-off from a city agency---requires
obtaining the alderman's blessing before formal papers are filed. This is not a
legal requirement but an operational reality: without the alderman's support, a
developer risks investing substantially in design, legal, and engineering work
before facing rejection at the political stage. One anonymous Chicago architect
described the dynamic: ``To get the alderman, you have to get the neighborhood
group, and that's where the trouble comes in. It's fraught with risk, even to have
this conversation.'' \citep{giangreco_game_2024}

\textit{Rezoning.} For projects requiring a change to the zoning code, the
alderman's role is effectively dispositive. Although zoning amendments formally
require a City Council vote, the council ``routinely shows unquestioning deference
to local aldermen even in the absence of any articulated local concern''
\citep{chase_chicago_2023}. Equally consequential is the alderman's ability to
preemptively \textit{downzone} parcels to foreclose future development. Joseph
Schwieterman, a DePaul University professor who studies Chicago land use, noted
that the practice ``creates real uncertainty for real estate developers when
downzoning becomes ad hoc or driven by the desire of an alderman to settle scores.''
\citep{gallun_ad_2019} Documented episodes include Ald.\ Brendan Reilly's
proposals to downzone a River North site where a developer had proposed a 60-story
tower and a separate site to block a 615-room hotel, and a Pilsen downzoning
orchestrated by Ald.\ Solis that stripped a parcel of its residential designation
to kill a planned 465-unit apartment project.

\textit{Permit processing.} For projects that proceed by-right under existing
zoning, the alderman retains substantial informal influence over the Department of
Buildings permit queue. The corruption case against Ald.\ Ed Burke illustrated the
mechanism in its most extreme form: federal prosecutors documented how his staff
withheld routine permits---including a driveway permit for a fast-food
restaurant---until the property owner directed legal business to Burke's law firm
\citep{spielman_aldermen_2019}. The case made vivid a dynamic that developers had long
understood: in Chicago, permit timing is a lever, and the lever sits in the
alderman's office.

\textit{Post-approval extraction.} Even after a project clears the formal permit
process, aldermen may condition their tacit support on design concessions, density
reductions, community benefit agreements, or contributions to ward amenities. These
negotiations leave no formal record but represent real costs to developers. A
Chicago developer summarized the climate: ``Doing business in Chicago is
significantly more challenging than other markets I work in.''
\citep{chicon_miguel_2025}

\paragraph{A recent example.} The Old Town Canvas project illustrates the full
pipeline in a single documented episode. Developer Fern Hill began community
outreach in 2021, proposing a roughly 500-unit mixed-income tower at a
transit-accessible site in the 2nd Ward, with approximately 100 affordable units
and an estimated \$2.5 million in annual property tax revenue. After four years and
more than ten community meetings, Ald.\ Brian Hopkins announced in January 2025
that he would not advance the project, citing opposition from residents of
neighboring high-rises. He was explicit about the source of his authority:
``Ultimately I am the decision maker on this. I'm not going to hide from that.
This is a zoning decision and in Chicago we have something called aldermanic
prerogative.'' \citep{achong_shortened_2025} Within two weeks the developer
resubmitted a scaled-down proposal---fewer floors, fewer units---and Hopkins
approved it. The episode captures both margins this paper studies: the approved
project is smaller than what the developer proposed (intensive margin), and the
four-year ordeal signals to other developers the cost of attempting large projects
in wards with active aldermanic gatekeeping (extensive margin).

\paragraph{Federal scrutiny.} The systemic consequences of this institution have
attracted federal attention. In October 2023, the U.S.\ Department of Housing and
Urban Development issued findings from a five-year investigation stemming from a
2018 complaint by the Chicago Area Fair Housing Alliance and nine other advocacy
groups. HUD concluded that aldermanic prerogative functions as an illegal ``local
veto'' over affordable housing proposals: ``the city affords each of its 50 wards a
local veto over proposals to build affordable housing and that many majority-white
wards use the local veto to block, deter or downsize such proposals''
\citep{chase_chicago_2023}. The question of whether to reform, limit, or
abolish aldermanic privilege remains a central issue in Chicago politics.\footnote{See
\url{https://news.wttw.com/elections/voters-guide/2023/races/city-council}, where
each city council candidate was asked specifically about their stance on aldermanic
prerogative.}

\subsection{Building Permits and the Permit Pipeline}

The building permits data come from the City of Chicago's open data portal and include all permit applications filed since 2006 with valid geographic coordinates and non-negative processing times. I distinguish between two categories of permits based on how much aldermanic discretion they are likely to face.

\textit{High-discretion permits} are those for new construction, renovation and alteration, wrecking and demolition, porch construction, and permits reinstated after being previously revoked.\footnote{Porch construction permits have received extra scrutiny since the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2003_Chicago_balcony_collapse}{2003 balcony collapse}.} These are the permit types most likely to draw community opposition and face delays due to community meetings or direct aldermanic intervention. \textit{Low-discretion permits} are those in the Easy Permit Process, Express Permit Program, Elevator Equipment, For Extension of PMT, and Scaffolding categories, excluding signs. These categories serve as a placebo comparison group because they are generally less exposed to discretionary aldermanic intervention than the high-discretion permits.

Distinguishing between these categories is crucial for isolating the alderman-specific component of the regulatory environment. High-discretion permits are the ones that require meaningful interaction with the alderman and are most likely to be affected by their behavior. Low-discretion permits serve as a useful comparison group: if the stringency scores are picking up something specific to the discretionary layer rather than general bureaucratic capacity, effects should concentrate in high-discretion permits and be absent for low-discretion ones.

The data bear this out. High-discretion permits take substantially longer to process and exhibit much more cross-alderman dispersion in mean processing time. At the permit level, longer processing times for high-discretion permits are associated with \textit{lower} ward-month permit volume, while low-discretion processing times are weakly positively correlated with ward-month permit volume, suggesting they reflect administrative capacity rather than regulatory friction. Figure~\ref{fig:permit_high_low_processing} visualizes these patterns; Appendix~\ref{app:permit_summary_stats} reports the corresponding summary statistics and volume correlations.

\subsection{Aldermanic Stringency Scores}
\label{subsec:strictness}

I construct the stringency score by residualizing permit processing times against ward and permit characteristics, then extracting the alderman-specific component. The first stage regresses log processing time at the permit level on ward-level demographic controls (median household income, racial composition, homeownership rate, population), permit-location geographic controls (distance to CBD, lakefront proximity, CTA rail access), lagged ward-month permit volume, and a rich set of fixed effects for year-month, permit type, and review type:
%
\begin{equation} \label{eq:strictness_reg1}
    \log(\text{Processing Time}_{i}) = \mathbf{X}_i \beta + \gamma_{\text{ym}} + \delta_{\text{pt}} + \zeta_{\text{rt}} + \epsilon_{i}
\end{equation}
%
where $\gamma_{\text{ym}}$ are year-month fixed effects, $\delta_{\text{pt}}$ are permit-type fixed effects, and $\zeta_{\text{rt}}$ are review-type fixed effects. Table~\ref{tab:stage1_residualization} in the Appendix reports the coefficients on the stage-one controls.

The second stage aggregates these residuals to the ward-month level and projects the ward-month mean residual onto alderman indicator variables and the same lagged ward-month permit volume control, weighted by the number of permits observed in each ward-month:
%
\begin{equation} \label{eq:strictness_reg2}
    \bar{\hat{\epsilon}}_{wm} = \mu + \alpha_{a} + \lambda \text{Volume}_{w,m-1} + \eta_{wm}
\end{equation}
%
The raw alderman fixed effects $\hat{\alpha}_a$ can be noisy for aldermen with few permits. I apply empirical Bayes shrinkage \citep{kline_systemic_2022, kline_discrimination_2024} to pull imprecise estimates toward the grand mean, then standardize the final scores to have mean zero and unit standard deviation.

Appendix~\ref{app:strictness_validation} reports additional validation. Higher stringency predicts fewer high-discretion permits and more evidence of regulatory friction in ward-month data, while the correlation between predecessor and successor aldermen within the same ward is relatively low ($\rho \approx 0.18$), supporting the interpretation that the index captures an alderman-specific component of the development process rather than a fixed ward trait.

Figure~\ref{fig:aldermanic_strictness_scores} shows the distribution of the final stringency scores, and Figure~\ref{fig:alderman_strictness_map} maps them across the city. There is score variation in wealthy and poor neighborhoods, on the North Side and South Side, confirming that the scores are not simply proxying for location or local demand conditions.

\subsection{New Construction Data}

I construct a dataset of all new residential construction in Chicago between 2006 and 2025 using publicly available data from the Cook County Assessor's Office. For buildings with up to six units, I use the residential improvement characteristics data, which provides building-level information on dwelling units, stories, and square footage. For larger multifamily buildings, I draw on the commercial valuation dataset, which covers properties assessed under multifamily model groups and provides unit counts, building area, and land area. I merge both sources with the Assessor's parcel universe for precise location coordinates, assign each parcel to its ward using City of Chicago shapefiles, and define the construction date as June 15 of the recorded year built. I keep parcels with positive lot area, positive building area, and at least one dwelling unit.

For each parcel I compute two standard density measures: Floor Area Ratio (FAR), defined as total building square footage divided by lot area, and Dwelling Units Per Acre (DUPAC), defined as dwelling units divided by lot acreage. Distance to the nearest ward boundary is computed in feet using the Illinois East State Plane coordinate system.

Table~\ref{tab:summary_stats} presents summary statistics. The full sample contains 14,014 new residential buildings. The 2,611 multifamily buildings (2+ units) are the focus of most of the density analysis. While I focus on multifamily buildings where aldermanic discretion has the most scope to matter, I also show results including all new construction and find similar patterns.

\subsection{Home Sales}

Home sales data come from the Cook County Assessor's Office and cover all residential transactions from 1999 to 2025. I restrict the sample to arm's-length transactions of single-family homes and small multifamily buildings (2--6 units), excluding nominal transfers, land-only sales, and intrafamily conveyances. Sale prices are first deflated to 2022 dollars using monthly Chicago CPI excluding shelter and then winsorized at the 1st and 99th percentiles. Each transaction is geocoded using the county parcel universe and assigned to the relevant ward based on the boundaries in effect at the sale date. Property-level hedonic controls (building square footage, land square footage, building age, bedrooms, bathrooms, garage) are drawn from a rolling join to the most recent assessor record as of the sale year.
