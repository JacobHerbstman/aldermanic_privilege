\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Housing costs in major American cities have risen sharply over the past two decades, and the most direct way to make housing more affordable is to build more of it. In dense urban areas where vacant land is scarce, that means building taller and denser. But building taller and denser requires navigating local land use regulations---and a growing body of evidence shows that these regulations restrict housing supply and raise prices \citep{glaeser_why_2005, saiz_geographic_2010, turner_land_2014, duranton_urban_2023}.

Most of this evidence comes from variation across municipalities. Less is known about how the fragmentation of land use authority \textit{within} a single city affects what gets built. This paper studies that question in the context of Chicago's ``aldermanic privilege'': the informal institution granting each of the city's 50 aldermen effective veto power over development in their ward. The institution has no basis in statute. It operates through norms: other aldermen defer to the local alderman on land use matters, and city agencies follow suit. The result is that each ward functions as a semi-independent regulatory jurisdiction, with the alderman acting as gatekeeper for anything that requires meaningful review.

The central finding of this paper is that aldermanic discretion reduces housing supply. The strongest evidence comes from two sources. First, a spatial discontinuity design comparing new construction on opposite sides of ward boundaries shows that multifamily development is 10--16\% less dense on the stricter side, within narrow bands where neighborhood characteristics are nearly identical. Second, when ward boundaries are redrawn and blocks are reassigned to stricter aldermen, the number of high-discretion permits issued on those blocks falls---a direct measure of how the development pipeline responds to a change in political representation. Together, these results show that aldermanic strictness affects both what gets proposed and what gets built.

I also present evidence on home prices. Using the same redistricting design, I find that home sale prices rise approximately 2\% when blocks are assigned to stricter aldermen. This is consistent with a supply restriction channel, but the price estimates are noisier and harder to separate cleanly from demand-side stories. I treat them as suggestive evidence that the supply effects documented earlier get capitalized into housing costs.

To study these questions I need a measure of how much friction each alderman imposes on development. I construct ``aldermanic strictness scores'' from permit processing times for high-discretion permits---the types that tend to draw community opposition and require aldermanic engagement. The construction proceeds in two steps: first, I residualize processing times against ward characteristics and time effects to remove variation driven by geography and demand; second, I apply empirical Bayes shrinkage \citep{kline_systemic_2022, kline_discrimination_2024} to address measurement error for aldermen with few permits. The resulting scores reveal substantial variation across aldermen that is not simply a reflection of where they represent.

The empirical strategy has three main components. The first is a cross-sectional comparison of development density at ward boundaries, using both a spatial regression discontinuity design and a segment fixed effects specification. The RD plots provide visual evidence that density drops sharply at the boundary when moving from the lenient to the strict side. The segment fixed effects regressions formalize this comparison, restricting the sample to parcels within 500 feet of ward boundaries and comparing density within the same boundary segment and zoning classification. The border-pair fixed effects specification, which pools across all segments of a given ward pair, tells the same story and serves as a robustness check.

The second component is a permit event study exploiting the 2015 ward redistricting. When boundaries are redrawn for population balancing, some blocks are reassigned to new aldermen while nearby blocks remain under the same representation. I compare high-discretion permit activity on reassigned versus non-reassigned blocks in a difference-in-differences framework. This design isolates the dynamic supply response: when a block is moved to a stricter alderman, does the development pipeline actually slow down? The answer is yes, and the effect shows up clearly for high-discretion permits while leaving low-discretion permits largely unaffected.

The third component is the home sales analysis. Using the same redistricting variation and a transaction-level border comparison design, I estimate how home prices respond to changes in aldermanic representation. The event study shows a gradual increase in prices on blocks reassigned to stricter aldermen, reaching roughly 2\% by five years post-redistricting. The effect is directionally consistent with theory and with the supply results, but the confidence intervals are wider and I cannot fully rule out demand-side channels.

A simple time-to-build framework organizes these results. The key idea is that aldermanic discretion operates like an effective delay on the development process: it pushes expected revenue further into the future and reduces its present value. In this setup, the regulatory environment enters through a single sufficient statistic---the product of the discount rate and the effective time-to-build---which determines both the density a developer chooses and the threshold at which a project becomes viable. Longer effective delays reduce project scale, flip marginal projects from ``build'' to ``don't build,'' reduce aggregate supply, and in equilibrium raise prices. The strictness score is an empirical proxy for this regulatory wedge.

This paper builds most directly on \citet{khan_decentralized_2021}, who uses FOIA-requested rezoning data to study the intensive margin of aldermanic privilege. He finds that rezonings are approximately 10\% smaller near ward boundaries where benefits spill over to adjacent wards. I study a broader margin: by examining all new construction and not just rezoned parcels, I capture the response of the entire development pipeline to aldermanic frictions---including projects that proceed by-right at reduced scale precisely to avoid discretionary review. The permit event study adds a dynamic dimension that the cross-sectional density evidence cannot provide.

More broadly, the paper contributes to the literature on how political fragmentation affects housing supply. Prior work has documented how fragmentation across municipalities leads to undersupply of housing relative to the social optimum \citep{hilber_origins_2013, kulka_how_2022, monarrez_dividing_2023, bordeu_commuting_2025, mast_warding_2024}. I show that fragmentation within a single city generates similar coordination failures. Chicago is nominally a unified municipality, but aldermanic privilege effectively decentralizes land use authority across 50 independent actors, each of whom internalizes the costs of development but not its citywide benefits.
