\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Housing costs in the United States have risen sharply, and a large literature points to land use regulation as a central cause. Restrictive zoning, minimum lot sizes, and density caps limit housing supply, raise prices, and distort the allocation of labor across cities \citep{glaeser_why_2005, saiz_geographic_2010, turner_land_2014, hsieh_housing_2019, duranton_urban_2023}. But the formal rules on the books are only part of the story. A growing body of work argues that the \textit{process} of navigating the regulatory system---including permitting delays, discretionary review, and uncertainty about approval---may matter as much as the rules themselves \citep{mayer_residential_2000, paciorek_supply_2013, molloy_housing_2022, soltas_how_2026}. When developers face long and unpredictable timelines, projects shrink, marginal investments become unviable, and housing supply falls \citep{cunningham_house_2006, bloom_impact_2009, oh_land_2024}. There is still relatively little causal evidence, however, on how the discretionary authority of local political actors shapes the development environment and housing supply.

This paper studies regulatory discretion in the context of Chicago's ``aldermanic privilege,'' the informal power of each of Chicago's 50 city council members (aldermen) to unilaterally block or approve development in their ward. Chicago has no city charter, so there is no statutory basis for this power. It operates through deference: other aldermen vote with the local member on land use matters, city agencies follow the local alderman's lead, and developers understand that meaningful projects require the alderman's blessing \citep{thale_aldermanic_2005, einstein_neighborhood_2019}. The result is a regulatory environment that changes discretely at ward boundaries. Two parcels on opposite sides of the same street, in the same neighborhood, with the same zoning, can face very different political gatekeepers depending on which alderman controls the ward. That creates unusually clean spatial variation in regulatory discretion, which I use to study how this institution shapes the development process.

To measure the regulatory environment each alderman creates, I construct ``aldermanic stringency scores'' from permit processing times for high-discretion permits. These are the permit types most likely to draw community opposition and aldermanic intervention: new construction, renovation, demolition, and similar categories that involve meaningful review. I residualize processing times against ward characteristics and time effects to strip out variation driven by geography and demand, then apply empirical Bayes shrinkage \citep{kline_systemic_2022, kline_discrimination_2024} to reduce noise for aldermen with relatively few permits. The resulting scores show large differences in regulatory stringency across Chicago's 50 wards that are not explained by location or demographics. This approach is motivated by evidence that permitting frictions are a primary determinant of housing supply elasticity \citep{paciorek_supply_2013, gyourko_local_2021, soltas_how_2026}, and by a simple time-to-build framework in which regulatory delay acts as a wedge on the present value of development \citep{kydland_time_1982, mayer_land_2000}.

I use these scores in two empirical designs. The first is a border discontinuity design comparing the density of new construction on opposite sides of ward boundaries, within narrow bands where neighborhood characteristics are nearly identical but the alderman changes. Using border-segment fixed effects at a 250-foot bandwidth, I find that a one standard deviation increase in aldermanic stringency is associated with about 13\% lower floor-area ratio and about 10\% lower dwelling units per acre in the full new-construction sample. In the multifamily subsample, the corresponding estimates are larger in magnitude but less precise. The effect is concentrated near ward boundaries and remains visible in a range of supporting checks.

The second design is an event study exploiting the 2015 ward redistricting. When ward boundaries are redrawn for population balancing, some census blocks are reassigned to new aldermen while nearby blocks remain under the same representation. Using within-census-block variation, I find that blocks moved to more stringent aldermen experience a 9\% decline in high-discretion permit issuance over the following five years. The effect is specific to the kinds of permits that require aldermanic engagement and is much smaller for the low-discretion permits (excluding signs) used as a placebo comparison group, which do not show a clear post-treatment pattern. This is direct evidence that the development pipeline responds when the regulatory gatekeeper changes.

Finally, I present suggestive evidence that the supply restrictions imposed by more stringent aldermen pass through into housing prices. Using the same redistricting variation, I find that home sale prices rise approximately 1\% on blocks reassigned to more stringent aldermen relative to nearby blocks that were not reassigned. The estimates are directionally consistent with the supply story, but they are noisier and harder to separate from demand-side explanations, so I interpret them cautiously.

This paper contributes most directly to the literature on how local political actors and institutions shape housing supply. \citet{khan_decentralized_2021} studies the same Chicago setting and finds that aldermen approve smaller rezonings near ward boundaries where benefits spill over to adjacent wards. I study a broader margin. By examining realized new construction rather than only statutory changes in allowed density through rezoned parcels, I capture the response of the full development pipeline to aldermanic frictions, including projects that proceed by right at reduced scale to avoid triggering discretionary review.

More broadly, the paper connects to several strands of the urban economics literature. \citet{paciorek_supply_2013} shows that regulation reduces housing supply primarily through its effect on new construction timelines. \citet{mayer_residential_2000} and \citet{mayer_land_2000} show that development lags are a first-order determinant of the supply response to demand shocks. \citet{soltas_how_2026} provides new evidence that permitting costs are large enough to materially affect housing affordability. My contribution is to show that variation in the discretionary layer of the regulatory process, beyond the formal rules themselves, generates economically meaningful differences in housing supply within a single city.

The paper also connects to the literature on how the fragmentation of decision-making authority leads to undersupply of housing. \citet{fischel_homevoter_2009} argues that homeowners use local political institutions to restrict development and protect property values. Subsequent work has documented how this plays out across municipalities, with each jurisdiction restricting development to a degree that may be locally rational but is collectively costly \citep{hilber_origins_2013, ortalo-magne_political_2014, kulka_how_2022, monarrez_dividing_2023, mast_warding_2024, bordeu_commuting_2025}. My results show that a similar coordination failure operates within a single city when informal authority is fragmented across local representatives, each of whom controls the regulatory environment in their own territory.
