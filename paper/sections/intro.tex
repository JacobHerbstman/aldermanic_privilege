\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Housing costs in the United States have risen sharply, and a large literature points to land use regulation as a central cause. Restrictive zoning, minimum lot sizes, and density caps limit housing supply, raise prices, and distort the allocation of labor across cities \citep{glaeser_why_2005, saiz_geographic_2010, turner_land_2014, hsieh_housing_2019, duranton_urban_2023}. Formal rules, however, are only one part of the regulatory environment. A growing body of work argues that the \textit{process} of navigating the regulatory system---including permitting delays, discretionary review, and uncertainty about approval---may matter as much as the rules themselves \citep{mayer_residential_2000, paciorek_supply_2013, molloy_housing_2022, soltas_how_2026}. When developers face long and unpredictable timelines, projects shrink, marginal investments become unviable, and housing supply falls \citep{cunningham_house_2006, bloom_impact_2009, oh_land_2024}. There is less direct evidence on how local political discretion shapes development and housing supply.

This paper studies regulatory discretion in the context of Chicago's ``aldermanic privilege,'' the informal power of each of Chicago's 50 city council members (aldermen) to unilaterally block or approve development in their ward. Chicago has no city charter, so there is no statutory basis for this power. It operates through deference: other aldermen vote with the local member on land use matters, city agencies follow the local alderman's lead, and developers understand that meaningful projects require the alderman's blessing \citep{thale_aldermanic_2005, einstein_neighborhood_2019}. This creates sharp changes in regulatory authority at ward boundaries. Two parcels on opposite sides of the same street, in the same neighborhood, with the same zoning, can face very different political gatekeepers depending on which alderman controls the ward. I use this boundary variation to study how aldermanic privilege affects development.

To measure the regulatory environment each alderman creates, I construct ``aldermanic stringency scores'' from permit processing times for high-discretion permits. These are the permit types most likely to draw community opposition and aldermanic intervention: new construction, renovation, demolition, and similar categories that involve meaningful review. I residualize processing times against ward characteristics and time effects to strip out variation driven by geography and demand, then apply empirical Bayes shrinkage \citep{kline_systemic_2022, kline_discrimination_2024} to reduce noise for aldermen with relatively few permits. The resulting scores reveal substantial cross-alderman variation that remains after controlling for location and ward demographics. This approach is motivated by evidence that permitting frictions are a primary determinant of housing supply elasticity \citep{paciorek_supply_2013, gyourko_local_2021, soltas_how_2026}, and by a simple time-to-build framework in which regulatory delay acts as a wedge on the present value of development \citep{kydland_time_1982, mayer_land_2000}.

The empirical analysis proceeds in two steps. First, I estimate effects on housing supply. A border design compares new construction on opposite sides of ward boundaries, within narrow bands where neighborhood characteristics are nearly identical but the alderman changes. Using border-segment fixed effects in the all-construction sample within 500ft of the boundary, I find that a one standard deviation increase in aldermanic stringency is associated with about 4\% lower floor-area ratio and 3\% lower dwelling units per acre. The multifamily estimates point in the same direction and are larger in magnitude, though estimated in a smaller sample.

I then use the 2015 ward redistricting to test whether permitting changes when a block receives a new alderman. Those specifications use a 1000ft window because they compare treated and control blocks within the same boundary neighborhoods over time rather than relying on a discontinuity exactly at the border. Using within-census-block variation over a symmetric 2010--2020 event window, I find that blocks moved to more stringent aldermen experience a 7.4\% decline in high-discretion permit issuance. The effect is specific to the kinds of permits that require aldermanic engagement and is much smaller for the low-discretion permits (excluding signs) used as a placebo comparison group, which do not show a clear post-treatment pattern.

I next examine housing costs near ward boundaries. Because RentHub listings begin in 2014, they cannot support a clean rent event study around the 2015 redistricting. I therefore use a common spatial RD design for both rents and home sales. In this design, rents are about 3\% higher on the stricter side after hedonic and amenity controls, and the estimate is larger in a location-stable rental sample. Home sale prices are about 1.5\% higher in the analogous 500ft sales RD. I treat these estimates as supporting evidence on housing costs; the paper's main evidence comes from density and permit outcomes.

The paper contributes most directly to work on local political control over housing supply. \citet{khan_decentralized_2021} studies the same Chicago setting and finds that aldermen approve smaller rezonings near ward boundaries where benefits spill over to adjacent wards. I study a broader margin. By examining realized new construction rather than only statutory changes in allowed density through rezoned parcels, I measure how realized construction responds to aldermanic frictions, including projects that proceed by right at reduced scale to avoid triggering discretionary review.

The paper also relates to work on permitting, time-to-build, and fragmented land-use authority. \citet{paciorek_supply_2013} shows that regulation reduces housing supply primarily through its effect on new construction timelines. \citet{mayer_residential_2000} and \citet{mayer_land_2000} show that development lags are a first-order determinant of the supply response to demand shocks. \citet{soltas_how_2026} provides new evidence that permitting costs are large enough to materially affect housing affordability. My contribution is to show that variation in the discretionary layer of the regulatory process, beyond the formal rules themselves, generates economically meaningful differences in housing supply within a single city.

The paper also connects to the literature on how the fragmentation of decision-making authority leads to undersupply of housing. \citet{fischel_homevoter_2009} argues that homeowners use local political institutions to restrict development and protect property values. Subsequent work has documented how this plays out across municipalities, with each jurisdiction restricting development to a degree that may be locally rational but is collectively costly \citep{hilber_origins_2013, ortalo-magne_political_2014, kulka_how_2022, monarrez_dividing_2023, mast_warding_2024, bordeu_commuting_2025}. My results show that a similar coordination failure operates within a single city when informal authority is fragmented across local representatives, each of whom controls the regulatory environment in their own territory.
