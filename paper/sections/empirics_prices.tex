\section{Price Evidence Near Ward Boundaries}
\label{sec:empirical_results_prices}

The supply results imply that housing costs may be higher on the more stringent side of ward boundaries.
However, the primary issue in housing supply debates is about affordability, so it is important to see whether these observed differences in housing supply translate into higher prices for residents.
To do this, I use the same border fixed effects design as in Section~\ref{sec:empirical_results_density} to compare rents and home sale prices on the more stringent versus more lenient side of the same ward boundary.
I compare observations within 500ft of the same ward boundary: on one side, residents face the alderman with the higher stringency score; across the boundary, they face the more lenient alderman, 
but the neighborhood is otherwise similar.

The estimating equation is
%
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:price_rd}
    \log(Y_{ist}) = \beta \cdot \text{StringencyIndex}_{is} + X_i'\gamma + \alpha_{s t} + \varepsilon_{ist},
\end{equation}
%
where $Y_{ist}$ is the real rent or real sale price for observation $i$ near boundary segment $s$ in period $t$, deflated to 2022 dollars with the monthly Chicago CPI-U all-items series.
The stringency index is the standardized score of the alderman representing the observation's side of the boundary.
The fixed effects are segment-by-month for rents and segment-by-quarter for home sales due to the lower frequency of home sale observations, and standard errors are clustered by boundary segment.
Since prices are determined by local supply and demand conditions and not by just the characteristics of the residential unit at any specific parcel, I do not include a linear distance trend in my main price specifications to get at average prices and rents within a local area around each ward border segment boundary.
The table reports the coefficient on the continuous stringency index measure.

\subsection{Rents}

The rental sample uses listed rents from 2014-2022.
I collapse repeated scrape rows to a floorplan-month panel before estimating the boundary comparison since RentHub does have known issues with repeated listings of the same unit.
The outcome is log listed rent in 2022 dollars.
Appendix Table~\ref{tab:rental_rd_controls_clean_location} repeats the analysis after dropping a small subset of listings whose locations appear to be unreliable near ward boundaries.

Figure~\ref{fig:rental_rd_flat} shows the boundary comparison visually.
The binned points show log listed rents after removing segment-by-month fixed effects, rental hedonic controls, and amenity controls.
Rents are noticeably higher on the more stringent side of the boundary and show about a $3\%$ jump.

Panel A of Table~\ref{tab:price_rd_controls} shows that the rent gap is not explained by observable listing characteristics or amenity controls.
Appendix Table~\ref{tab:rental_rd_paired_amenity_balance} compares external amenities on the less and more stringent sides of the same rental boundary segments.
The few marginal differences are small in magnitude, so nearby amenities are unlikely to explain much of the rent gap.
A one-standard-deviation increase in aldermanic stringency is associated with 2.49\% higher listed rents without controls, 1.81\% higher listed rents with hedonic controls, and 1.84\% higher listed rents after adding distances to schools, park-boundary polygons, major streets, CTA stops open by listing month, and Lake Michigan.
So at most, mechnical differences in rental-unit composition and neighborhood amenities can explain about a quarter of the observed rent gap.

\subsection{Home Sales}

I estimate the analogous boundary comparison for home sales from 2006-2022, since the data go back further for official Cook County datasets.
The outcome is log sale price, deflated to 2022 dollars as well.
The sales table uses the same continuous stringency-index specification as the rental table, with segment-by-quarter fixed effects and standard errors clustered by boundary segment.
Hedonic controls include building area, land area, building age, bedrooms, bathrooms, and garage presence.
Amenity controls are parcel-level distances to the nearest school, Chicago Park District park boundary, major street, and Lake Michigan.

Figure~\ref{fig:sales_rd_flat} plots the regression discontinuity estimate visually and there is a smaller but noticeable $1.5\%$ jump in sale prices on the more stringent side of the boundary within a border segment and year-quarter.

Panel B of Table~\ref{tab:price_rd_controls} reports the corresponding estimates for the continuous stringency index specification.
A one-standard-deviation increase in aldermanic stringency is associated with about 1.2\% higher sale prices across the three specifications.
The estimates are smaller than the rent estimates and are only marginally significant after adding controls, but suggest that home prices along with rental rates respond to the regulatory environment.

As a placebo check, Appendix Figures~\ref{fig:rent_placebo_boundaries} and~\ref{fig:sales_placebo_boundaries} repeat the rent and sales plots after shifting the cutoff 1000ft inside each side of the boundary.
Each shifted cutoff uses the same 500ft window, so the placebo windows do not overlap the main true-boundary sample.
As with the density placebo checks, we see no equivalent jump in rents or sale prices at these placebo boundaries, which suggests we are not just picking up unobserved neighborhood differences that happen to correlate with the ward boundaries.

\subsection{Rental Listing Supply}

Motivated by the inability of observable unit characteristics or neighborhood amenities to fully explain the rent and price gaps, 
I ask whether observed supply differences in the rental market could be driving the rent gap.
While units within a border segment are close to each other and one may expect rents to equalize across the boundary, \citet{asquith_local_2023}
show that rents are very local and can vary in their response to new supply within a few neighborhood blocks.

I repeat the same boundary-segment comparison using the count of listed floorplans in the rental data as the outcome, which proxies for supply differences across the boundary in RentHub's scraped listings.
I once again use PPML to estimate the effect on a count variable, with segment-by-month fixed effects and standard errors clustered by boundary segment.
While the measure is imprecise and not statistically distinguishable from zero, Figure~\ref{fig:rental_supply_rd} shows about 10\% fewer observed floorplan-months on the more stringent side of the boundary (PPML estimate $=-10.2\%$, SE $=7.3$).
Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:rental_supply_rd_clean_location} repeats the same exercise after dropping coordinate-risk listings; the estimate remains similar at $-8.6\%$ (SE $=7.7$).
The pattern is consistent with the observed rent gap reflecting lower local rental supply on the more stringent side of the boundary.
