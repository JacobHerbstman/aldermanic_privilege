\section{Density at Ward Boundaries}
\label{sec:empirical_results_density}

\subsection{Visual Evidence}

If aldermanic discretion affects the development process, it should show up in what gets built near ward boundaries, where neighborhood characteristics are similar but the alderman changes. I begin with border discontinuity plots that pool parcels near ward boundaries across the city, placing the more lenient alderman's side at negative distances and the more stringent side at positive distances. The main sample includes all new construction within 250 feet of a ward boundary, and the lower panels report the same display for the multifamily subsample. I residualize density outcomes against zoning-group fixed effects\footnote{I group Chicago zoning codes into the following categories: RS-* single-family residential; RT-* and RM-* multi-family residential; B-* neighborhood mixed-use; C-* commercial; M-* industrial; DX-*, DR-*, DS-*, and DC-* downtown; PD planned development; all remaining codes Other.}, boundary-segment fixed effects, construction-year fixed effects, and own-side demographic controls before plotting. This removes broad zoning differences, location along the boundary, and citywide trends before tracing out the local linear relationship in running distance on each side of the cutoff.

Figure~\ref{fig:rd_fe_plots} presents the results using a 250-foot bandwidth. In the full new-construction sample, both log(FAR) and log(DUPAC) fall when moving from the lenient to the stringent side of the boundary.%
\footnote{The displayed estimate comes from an unweighted local-linear regression of the logged outcome on a boundary-side indicator, running distance, and their interaction, with zoning-group, segment, and year fixed effects plus own-side demographic controls. Standard errors are clustered at the ward-pair level.}
In the multifamily subsample, the pattern has the same sign but wider confidence bands because relatively few multifamily projects lie very close to the boundary. The plots are best read as a visual summary of the discontinuity itself: denser projects appear on the more lenient side, and the break occurs where jurisdiction changes. The regression analysis that follows uses continuous variation in aldermanic stringency within boundary segments to estimate how much of that discontinuity lines up with measured aldermanic stringency.

\subsection{Border Fixed Effects Regressions}

To move from visual evidence to a formal estimate, I compare density within narrow boundary segments controlling for zoning, time, and smooth distance-to-border gradients:
%
\begin{equation} \label{eq:segment_fe}
    \ln(Y_{iwst}) = \beta \cdot \text{Stringency}_{w} + \lambda_L D^-_{iwst} + \lambda_S D^+_{iwst} + X_i'\theta + \alpha_{\text{zone group}} + \alpha_{\text{segment}} + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{iwst}
\end{equation}
%
where $Y_{iwst}$ is a density outcome for parcel $i$ in ward $w$ on boundary segment $s$, built in year $t$; $D^-_{iwst}$ and $D^+_{iwst}$ are side-specific distances to the ward boundary on the lenient and stringent sides; and $X_i$ collects own-side demographic controls. The fixed effects enter additively: zoning-group fixed effects absorb differences across broad zoning environments, segment fixed effects absorb differences across boundary segments, and year fixed effects absorb citywide trends. The main sample uses new construction within 250 feet of a ward boundary. Panel B of the table reports the multifamily subsample at the same bandwidth. Standard errors are clustered at the ward-pair level. Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:pruned_border_segments} maps the boundary segments that enter the analysis.

The segment fixed effects are the central design element. Boundary segments are short geographic units, much shorter than full ward-pair borders that can stretch for miles through different neighborhoods, so comparisons within segments are spatially precise. The side-specific distance terms absorb smooth within-side gradients in development density as one moves toward or away from the boundary. Zoning-group fixed effects ensure that parcels are compared within broad zoning environments rather than across fundamentally different parts of the zoning code.

Table~\ref{tab:density_main_table} presents the results for the two main density outcomes, DUPAC and FAR. Panel A is the cleanest specification: in the full new-construction sample, a one standard deviation increase in aldermanic stringency is associated with lower development density on both margins, with the FAR estimate more precisely estimated than the DUPAC estimate. Panel B reports the multifamily subsample at the same 250-foot bandwidth. Those coefficients are larger in magnitude, but they are also much noisier.

\subsection{Robustness}
\label{subsec:robustness}

The density results are robust to a range of specification checks, which I summarize here and report in full in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_density}.

I begin with placebo tests that move the cutoff 250 feet into each ward. If the discontinuity is genuinely driven by the boundary where aldermanic jurisdiction changes, it should not appear at arbitrary locations within wards. Figure~\ref{fig:rd_placebo} shifts the cutoff 250 feet into each ward and re-estimates the local-linear discontinuity for the all-construction sample. Relative to the true boundary, the placebo plots are flatter around the shifted cutoffs and do not show the same visible break in either FAR or DUPAC.

The appendix reports several additional checks. Excluding parcels within 25 or 50 feet of the boundary leaves the local-linear discontinuity negative in the all-construction sample (Figure~\ref{fig:rd_donut}). Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_density} also reports the multifamily sample at a 500-foot bandwidth, a ward-pair fixed-effects version of that specification (Table~\ref{tab:fe_pair_year}), and a pruned version of the main all-construction specification that drops ward-pair-era combinations flagged as potentially confounded by simultaneous institutional changes (Table~\ref{tab:fe_pruned}). It also shows that the multifamily discontinuity is more pronounced for ward pairs with larger strictness gaps (Figure~\ref{fig:rd_gap_split}).
