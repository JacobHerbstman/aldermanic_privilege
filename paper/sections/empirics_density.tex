\section{Density at Ward Boundaries}
\label{sec:empirical_results_density}

\subsection{Visual Evidence}

If aldermanic discretion affects the development process, its effects should be visible in what actually gets built near ward boundaries, where neighborhood characteristics are nearly identical but the alderman changes. I begin with border discontinuity plots that show this relationship directly. I stack all ward boundaries together, placing the more lenient alderman's side at negative distances and the more stringent side at positive distances. The sample is restricted to new multifamily construction, and I residualize density outcomes against zoning-group fixed effects\footnote{I group Chicago zoning codes into the following categories: RS-* single-family residential; RT-* and RM-* multi-family residential; B-* neighborhood mixed-use; C-* commercial; M-* industrial; DX-*, DR-*, DS-*, and DC-* downtown; PD planned development; all remaining codes Other.}, boundary-segment fixed effects, construction-year fixed effects, and own-side demographic controls before plotting. This removes variation from broad zoning differences, location along the boundary, and citywide trends.

Figure~\ref{fig:rd_fe_plots} presents the results using a 500-foot bandwidth. Both log(FAR) and log(DUPAC) show a clear downward discontinuity at the boundary when moving from the lenient to the stringent side.%
\footnote{The jump estimate is from an unweighted linear regression of the residualized outcome on a boundary-side indicator, running distance, and their interaction, with own-side demographic controls and standard errors clustered at the ward-pair level. Exact point estimates and standard errors appear in the figure subtitles.}

These magnitudes are large, and they should be. The border discontinuity captures the total equilibrium difference in density, which reflects both the direct effect of aldermanic behavior and developer self-selection into more lenient wards. Developers can choose where to build, and they clearly prefer the lenient side when all else is equal. The plots establish that something changes sharply at ward boundaries. The regression analysis that follows isolates how much of that change is attributable to aldermanic stringency.

\subsection{Border Fixed Effects Regressions}

To move from visual evidence to a formal estimate, I compare density within narrow boundary segments controlling for zoning and time effects:
%
\begin{equation} \label{eq:segment_fe}
    \ln(Y_{iwst}) = \beta \cdot \text{Stringency}_{w} + X_i'\theta + \alpha_{\text{zone group}} + \alpha_{\text{segment}} + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{iwst}
\end{equation}
%
where $Y_{iwst}$ is a density outcome for parcel $i$ in ward $w$ on boundary segment $s$, built in year $t$, and $X_i$ collects own-side demographic controls. The fixed effects enter additively: zoning-group fixed effects absorb differences across broad zoning environments, segment fixed effects absorb differences across boundary segments, and year fixed effects absorb citywide trends. The sample is restricted to new multifamily construction within 500 feet of a ward boundary. Standard errors are clustered at the ward-pair level. Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:pruned_border_segments} maps the boundary segments that enter the analysis.

The segment fixed effects are the key design element. Boundary segments are short geographic units, much shorter than full ward-pair borders that can stretch for miles through different neighborhoods, so comparisons within segments are spatially precise. Zoning-group fixed effects ensure that parcels are compared within broad zoning environments rather than across fundamentally different parts of the zoning code.

Table~\ref{tab:segment_fe_500} presents the results for the two main density outcomes, DUPAC and FAR. A one standard deviation increase in aldermanic stringency is associated with about 10\% lower FAR and DUPAC. To test whether the stringency score is picking up smooth variation in neighborhood characteristics at the boundary, I run balance tests using the same specification on block group-level demographics from the 2019 ACS. I find null results across racial composition, median income, education, homeownership, median rent, home values, and population density (see Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_density}), which supports the interpretation that the density effects reflect aldermanic behavior rather than sorting on observables.

\subsection{Robustness}
\label{subsec:robustness}

The density results are robust to a range of specification checks, which I summarize here with full results in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_density}.

The most informative diagnostic is the median-gap split. The border discontinuity plots pool all ward boundaries together, which raises a natural concern that the discontinuity is driven entirely by boundaries where the gap in aldermanic stringency is especially large. Figure~\ref{fig:rd_median_split} splits the sample at the median stringency gap and estimates the discontinuity separately for each subsample. The jump is present on both sides of the median, though it is larger and more precisely estimated for above-median gaps, as one would expect if the treatment is real and the dose matters.

A placebo test provides further reassurance. If the discontinuity is genuinely driven by the ward boundary where aldermanic jurisdiction changes, it should not appear at arbitrary locations within wards. Figure~\ref{fig:rd_placebo} shifts the cutoff 500 feet into each ward and re-estimates the discontinuity. Neither placebo cutoff shows a significant jump, confirming that the density drop is specific to the actual boundary.

The appendix reports several additional checks. A donut specification that excludes parcels within 25 or 50 feet of the boundary addresses concerns that parcels at the exact cutoff are unusual or mismeasured(Figure~\ref{fig:rd_donut}), and the effect persists. Replacing segment fixed effects with coarser ward-pair fixed effects still yields negative estimates, though they are slightly attenuated relative to the segment-fixed-effects baseline (Table~\ref{tab:fe_pair_year}), as does dropping ward-pair-era combinations flagged as potentially confounded by simultaneous institutional changes (Table~\ref{tab:fe_pruned}). Narrowing the bandwidth to 250 feet produces comparable estimates with wider confidence intervals (Table~\ref{tab:fe_estimates_250}), while widening it to 1,000 feet attenuates the effects (Table~\ref{tab:fe_estimates_1000}), consistent with aldermanic influence being concentrated near ward boundaries. Finally, extending the sample to include all new construction, not just multifamily, yields a smaller but still visible discontinuity (Figure~\ref{fig:rd_all_units}), as expected given that single-family construction has less variation in density and are less likely to be the subject of community meetings, unit count negotiations, or other frictions the aldermen could impose.
