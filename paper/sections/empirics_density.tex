\section{Density at Ward Boundaries}
\label{sec:empirical_results_density}

\subsection{Visual Evidence}

If aldermanic discretion affects the development process, its effects should be visible in what actually gets built near ward boundaries, where neighborhood characteristics are similar but the alderman changes. I begin with border discontinuity plots that stack all ward boundaries together, placing the more lenient alderman's side at negative distances and the more stringent side at positive distances. The sample is restricted to new multifamily construction, and I residualize density outcomes against zoning-group fixed effects\footnote{I group Chicago zoning codes into the following categories: RS-* single-family residential; RT-* and RM-* multi-family residential; B-* neighborhood mixed-use; C-* commercial; M-* industrial; DX-*, DR-*, DS-*, and DC-* downtown; PD planned development; all remaining codes Other.}, boundary-segment fixed effects, construction-year fixed effects, and own-side demographic controls before plotting. This removes broad zoning differences, location along the boundary, and citywide trends before tracing out the local linear relationship in running distance on each side of the cutoff.

Figure~\ref{fig:rd_fe_plots} presents the results using a 500-foot bandwidth. Both log(FAR) and log(DUPAC) show a clear downward discontinuity at the boundary when moving from the lenient to the stringent side.%
\footnote{The displayed estimate comes from an unweighted local-linear regression of the logged outcome on a boundary-side indicator, running distance, and their interaction, with zoning-group, segment, and year fixed effects plus own-side demographic controls. Standard errors are clustered at the ward-pair level.}
The plots are useful as a visual summary of the border discontinuity itself: denser projects appear on the more lenient side, and the break occurs precisely where jurisdiction changes. The regression analysis that follows uses continuous variation in aldermanic stringency within boundary segments to estimate how much of that discontinuity lines up with measured aldermanic stringency.

\subsection{Border Fixed Effects Regressions}

To move from visual evidence to a formal estimate, I compare density within narrow boundary segments controlling for zoning, time, and smooth distance-to-border gradients:
%
\begin{equation} \label{eq:segment_fe}
    \ln(Y_{iwst}) = \beta \cdot \text{Stringency}_{w} + \lambda_L D^-_{iwst} + \lambda_S D^+_{iwst} + X_i'\theta + \alpha_{\text{zone group}} + \alpha_{\text{segment}} + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{iwst}
\end{equation}
%
where $Y_{iwst}$ is a density outcome for parcel $i$ in ward $w$ on boundary segment $s$, built in year $t$; $D^-_{iwst}$ and $D^+_{iwst}$ are side-specific distances to the ward boundary on the lenient and stringent sides; and $X_i$ collects own-side demographic controls. The fixed effects enter additively: zoning-group fixed effects absorb differences across broad zoning environments, segment fixed effects absorb differences across boundary segments, and year fixed effects absorb citywide trends. The sample is restricted to new multifamily construction within 500 feet of a ward boundary. Standard errors are clustered at the ward-pair level. Appendix Figure~\ref{fig:pruned_border_segments} maps the boundary segments that enter the analysis.

The segment fixed effects are the central design element. Boundary segments are short geographic units, much shorter than full ward-pair borders that can stretch for miles through different neighborhoods, so comparisons within segments are spatially precise. The side-specific distance terms absorb smooth within-side gradients in development density as one moves toward or away from the boundary. Zoning-group fixed effects ensure that parcels are compared within broad zoning environments rather than across fundamentally different parts of the zoning code.

Table~\ref{tab:segment_fe_500} presents the results for the two main density outcomes, DUPAC and FAR. A one standard deviation increase in aldermanic stringency is associated with lower development density on both margins.

\subsection{Robustness}
\label{subsec:robustness}

The density results are robust to a range of specification checks, which I summarize here with full results in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_density}.

I first provide a placebo test by moving the cutoff 500 feet into each ward. If the discontinuity is genuinely driven by the ward boundary where aldermanic jurisdiction changes, it should not appear at arbitrary locations within wards. Figure~\ref{fig:rd_placebo} shifts the cutoff 500 feet into each ward and re-estimates the local-linear discontinuity. Relative to the true boundary, the placebo plots are flatter around the shifted cutoffs and do not show the same visible break.

The appendix reports several additional checks. Excluding parcels within 25 or 50 feet of the boundary leaves the local-linear discontinuity negative (Figure~\ref{fig:rd_donut}). Replacing segment fixed effects with coarser ward-pair fixed effects still yields negative estimates, though they are attenuated relative to the segment-fixed-effects baseline (Table~\ref{tab:fe_pair_year}), as does dropping ward-pair-era combinations flagged as potentially confounded by simultaneous institutional changes (Table~\ref{tab:fe_pruned}). Narrowing the bandwidth to 250 feet produces comparable estimates with wider confidence intervals (Table~\ref{tab:fe_estimates_250}), consistent with aldermanic influence being concentrated near ward boundaries. Finally, extending the sample to include all new construction, not just multifamily, produces a smaller plotted discontinuity (Figure~\ref{fig:rd_all_units}), as expected given that single-family construction has less variation in density and is less likely to be the subject of community meetings, unit count negotiations, or other frictions aldermen could impose.
