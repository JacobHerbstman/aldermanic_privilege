\section{Cross-Sectional Evidence: Density at Ward Boundaries}
\label{sec:empirical_results_density}

This section asks whether stricter aldermen reduce the density of new construction in their wards. I answer it using comparisons of multifamily development on opposite sides of ward boundaries, where neighborhood characteristics are nearly identical but the alderman---and the regulatory environment---changes discretely.

\subsection{Visual Evidence}

I begin with spatial RD plots that show the relationship between distance to the ward boundary and development density. I stack all ward boundaries together, placing the more lenient alderman's side at negative distances and the stricter side at positive distances. The sample is restricted to new multifamily construction (2--100 units) where both sides of the boundary share the same zoning classification, so differences in density cannot be mechanically driven by differences in allowable FAR.

Figure~\ref{fig:rd_fe_plots} presents FE-adjusted versions of these plots. Residualizing density against zoning-group, segment, and construction-year fixed effects removes variation driven by zoning differences, location along the boundary, and citywide trends, isolating the spatial pattern around the boundary itself. Using a 500-foot bandwidth and triangular kernel weights, both log(FAR) and log(DUPAC) show a clear downward discontinuity at the boundary when moving from the lenient to the strict side. The bias-corrected estimates from rdrobust \citep{calonico_rdrobust_2015} indicate a roughly 50\% decrease in FAR and a 29\% decrease in DUPAC at the cutoff.

These magnitudes are large, and they should be. The RD captures the total equilibrium difference in density, which reflects both the direct effect of aldermanic behavior and developer self-selection into more lenient wards. Developers can choose where to build, and they clearly prefer the lenient side when all else is equal. The plots establish that something changes sharply at ward boundaries---the regression analysis that follows isolates how much of that change is attributable to aldermanic strictness.

\subsection{Border Fixed Effects Regressions}

To move from visual evidence to a formal estimate, I compare density within narrow boundary segments, controlling for zoning and time effects. The estimating equation is:
%
\begin{equation} \label{eq:segment_fe}
    \ln(Y_{iwst}) = \beta \cdot \text{Strictness}_{w} + \alpha_{\text{zone group}} + \alpha_{\text{segment}} + \gamma_t + \varepsilon_{iwst}
\end{equation}
%
where $Y_{iwst}$ is a density outcome for parcel $i$ in ward $w$ on boundary segment $s$, built in year $t$. The fixed effects enter additively: $\alpha_{\text{zone group}}$ absorbs differences across zoning classifications, $\alpha_{\text{segment}}$ absorbs differences across boundary segments, and $\gamma_t$ absorbs citywide trends. The sample is restricted to new multifamily construction (2--100 units) within 500 feet of a ward boundary. Standard errors are clustered at the ward-pair level.

The segment fixed effects ensure that comparisons are made between parcels on opposite sides of the same boundary segment. Boundary segments are short geographic units---shorter than full ward-pair borders, which can stretch for miles through different neighborhoods---so comparisons within segments are spatially precise. Zoning-group fixed effects ensure that a parcel in an RT-3.5 zone (max FAR 1.05) is not compared to one in an RM-5.5 zone (max FAR 2.5), which would generate mechanical density differences unrelated to aldermanic behavior.

The three density outcomes are standard: Dwelling Units Per Acre (DUPAC), Floor Area Ratio (FAR), and total units in the building. $\text{Strictness}_w$ is the standardized aldermanic strictness score described in Section~\ref{subsec:strictness}.

Table~\ref{tab:segment_fe_500} presents the results. A one standard deviation increase in aldermanic strictness is associated with approximately 10\% lower density across all three measures. The effects are substantial and statistically significant.

To test whether aldermanic strictness is picking up smooth variation in neighborhood characteristics across the boundary, I run balance tests using the same specification. I assign each parcel to a census block group via spatial intersection with 2019 ACS geographies and regress eight demographic variables---racial composition, median income, education, homeownership, median rent, home values, and population density---on the strictness score. I find null results across all variables (see Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_density}), supporting the interpretation that the density effects are driven by aldermanic behavior rather than sorting on observables.

\subsection{Robustness}
\label{subsec:robustness}

The density results are robust to a range of alternative specifications and diagnostic tests. I summarize the main checks here; full results are in Appendix~\ref{sec:appendix_density}.

\paragraph{Above and below median strictness gap.} The RD plots pool all ward boundaries together. One natural concern is that the discontinuity is driven entirely by boundaries where the gap in aldermanic strictness is especially large. Figure~\ref{fig:rd_median_split} splits the sample at the median strictness gap and estimates the RD separately for above-median and below-median boundaries. The discontinuity is present in both subsamples, though it is larger and more precisely estimated for above-median gaps, as one would expect if the treatment is real and the dose matters.

\paragraph{Placebo boundaries.} If the discontinuity is genuinely driven by the ward boundary---where aldermanic jurisdiction changes---it should not appear at other locations. Figure~\ref{fig:rd_placebo} shifts the cutoff 500 feet into each ward and re-estimates the RD. Neither placebo cutoff shows a significant discontinuity, confirming that the density drop is specific to the actual boundary.

\paragraph{Donut specifications.} To address concerns that parcels precisely at the boundary are unusual---for example, if boundaries follow major roads that independently affect development---I exclude parcels within 25 or 50 feet of the boundary and re-estimate. The discontinuity persists (Figure~\ref{fig:rd_donut} in the Appendix).

\paragraph{Alternative fixed effects and bandwidths.} The Appendix also reports regressions using ward-pair fixed effects instead of segment fixed effects (Table~\ref{tab:fe_pair_year}), which pools across all segments of a given ward pair. The results are similar. Regressions at 250-foot and 1,000-foot bandwidths (Tables~\ref{tab:fe_estimates_250} and \ref{tab:fe_estimates_1000}) show attenuation at wider bandwidths, consistent with the effects being concentrated near the boundary where aldermanic jurisdiction changes.

\paragraph{All construction.} The main analysis focuses on multifamily buildings (2--100 units), where aldermanic discretion has the most scope to matter. Figure~\ref{fig:rd_all_units} in the Appendix shows the RD including all new construction. The discontinuity is smaller but still present, as expected given that single-family construction has less variation in density.
