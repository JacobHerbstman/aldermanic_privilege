\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper studies how informal regulatory discretion over development affects housing supply. I focus on Chicago's aldermanic privilege, the informal power of each of the city's 50 aldermen to unilaterally block or approve development in their ward, and show that it has measurable effects on development activity in the city.

Using permit processing times for high-discretion permits, I construct aldermanic stringency scores that isolate the alderman-specific component of regulatory delay after removing variation attributable to ward characteristics. A border discontinuity design shows that new construction is less dense on the more stringent side of ward boundaries: in 100m border-segment regressions, a one standard deviation increase in aldermanic stringency lowers floor-area ratio by about 10\% and dwelling units per acre by about 9\%. Event studies exploiting ward redistricting, estimated within 250m of ward boundaries, show that when blocks are reassigned to more stringent aldermen, high-discretion permit issuance falls by approximately 7\%, while low-discretion permits that bypass aldermanic review show a much smaller and less precisely estimated response. Home sale prices rise modestly on blocks assigned to more stringent aldermen, though this evidence is exploratory and noisier.

The paper provides causal evidence that variation in informal discretionary authority, beyond the formal rules on the books, generates economically meaningful differences in housing supply within a single city. The results fit a broader literature showing that permitting frictions and time-to-build are important determinants of supply elasticity \citep{paciorek_supply_2013, soltas_how_2026}. The policy implication is straightforward: reforms to the structure of land use decision-making in Chicago could materially affect housing production. But as the institutional evidence in Section~\ref{sec:data} makes clear, aldermen control both the permitting process and the zoning code, so effective reform likely needs to address both channels.
