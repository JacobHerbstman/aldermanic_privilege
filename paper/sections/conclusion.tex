\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper studies how informal regulatory discretion over development affects housing supply. I focus on Chicago's aldermanic privilege, the informal power of each of the city's 50 aldermen to unilaterally block or approve development in their ward, and show that it has quantifiable effects on development activity in the city.

Using permit processing times for high-discretion permits, I construct aldermanic stringency scores that isolate the alderman-specific component of regulatory delay after removing variation attributable to ward characteristics. A border discontinuity design shows that new multifamily construction is roughly 10\% less dense on the more stringent side of ward boundaries, in comparisons that absorb zoning classification, boundary location, and construction year. An event study exploiting ward redistricting shows that when blocks are reassigned to more stringent aldermen, high-discretion permit issuance falls by approximately 9\%, while low-discretion permits that bypass aldermanic review are unaffected. Home sale prices rise modestly on blocks assigned to more stringent aldermen, consistent with supply restrictions being capitalized into housing costs, though this evidence is noisier.

The paper provides causal evidence that variation in informal discretionary authority, not just the formal rules on the books, generates economically meaningful differences in housing supply within a single city. This complements the broader finding that permitting frictions and time-to-build are primary determinants of supply elasticity \citep{paciorek_supply_2013, soltas_how_2026}. The policy implication is that reforms targeting the structure of land use decision-making in Chicago could meaningfully affect housing production, but as the institutional evidence in Section~\ref{sec:data} documents, aldermen control both the permitting process and the zoning code, and effective reform likely needs to address both channels.
