\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper studies how the fragmentation of land use authority within a city affects housing supply. I focus on Chicago's aldermanic privilege---the informal institution giving each of the city's 50 aldermen effective veto power over development in their ward---and show that it has quantifiable effects on the development process.

I construct aldermanic strictness scores from permit processing times, using residualization and empirical Bayes shrinkage to isolate the component of regulatory delay attributable to individual aldermen. These scores reveal substantial variation in how much friction each alderman imposes on development, variation that is not simply a reflection of geography or demographics.

Three pieces of evidence establish the supply effects of aldermanic discretion. First, new multifamily construction is 10--16\% less dense on the stricter side of ward boundaries, in comparisons that hold zoning, location, and construction year constant. Second, when blocks are reassigned to stricter aldermen through redistricting, high-discretion permit issuance falls by approximately 9\%---a direct measure of how the development pipeline responds to a change in political representation. Third, home prices rise roughly 2\% on blocks assigned to stricter aldermen, consistent with supply restrictions getting capitalized into housing costs, though this evidence is more suggestive.

The results demonstrate that within-city fragmentation of land use authority generates coordination failures similar to those documented across municipalities. Each alderman, rationally responding to the preferences of their constituents, restricts development to a degree that may be locally optimal but is collectively costly. The effects are concentrated in precisely the types of permits that require aldermanic engagement, and they appear at the fine spatial scales where aldermanic jurisdiction changes---at ward boundaries and on individual blocks that switch aldermen.

Several limitations are worth noting. The estimates capture the intensive and realized-development margins but not the deterrence margin of projects never proposed, and so represent lower bounds. The analysis is partial-equilibrium and does not capture citywide general-equilibrium adjustments. And while the permit event study cleanly identifies the supply channel, the home price effects are harder to separate from demand-side mechanisms.

Future work could quantify the welfare costs of aldermanic privilege using a structural model of developer and household location choice, examine whether strictness affects the composition of housing supply (particularly affordable versus market-rate units), and test whether transparency requirements or electoral competition discipline restrictive behavior. Extending the approach to other cities with ward-based governance would help assess external validity.

Chicago's debate over aldermanic privilege is not abstract. The institution shapes where housing gets built and how much it costs. The evidence here suggests that reforming fragmented land use authority---through some combination of by-right expansion, centralization, or transparency---could meaningfully increase housing supply in a city that needs it.
