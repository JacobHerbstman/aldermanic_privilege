\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper studies how informal regulatory discretion over development affects housing supply. I focus on Chicago's aldermanic privilege, the informal power of each of the city's 50 aldermen to unilaterally block or approve development in their ward, and show that it has quantifiable effects on what gets built and how much it costs.

Using permit processing times for high-discretion permits, I construct aldermanic stringency scores that isolate the alderman-specific component of regulatory delay after removing variation attributable to ward characteristics. These scores reveal substantial heterogeneity in the regulatory environment across wards that is not explained by geography or demographics. A border discontinuity design shows that new multifamily construction is roughly 10\% less dense on the more stringent side of ward boundaries, in comparisons that absorb zoning-group, boundary-segment, and construction-year fixed effects plus own-side demographic controls. An event study exploiting ward redistricting shows that when blocks are reassigned to more stringent aldermen, high-discretion permit issuance falls by approximately 9\%, while the corresponding low-discretion placebo outcome is small and statistically insignificant. Home sale prices rise modestly on blocks assigned to more stringent aldermen, consistent with supply restrictions being capitalized into housing costs, though this evidence is noisier and more suggestive.

The results have implications both for the regulatory process literature and for Chicago-specific policy debates. On the research side, the paper provides causal evidence that variation in informal discretionary authority, not just the formal rules on the books, generates economically meaningful differences in housing supply within a single city. This complements the broader finding that permitting frictions and time-to-build are primary determinants of supply elasticity \citep{paciorek_supply_2013, soltas_how_2026}. On the policy side, the results suggest that reforms targeting the structure of land use decision-making in Chicago could meaningfully affect housing production, but that effective reform likely needs to address both the permitting process and aldermanic control over the zoning code itself.

Several limitations warrant mention. The estimates capture the intensive margin and the extensive margin of realized development but not the deterrence margin of projects never proposed, and so represent lower bounds on the total supply impact. The analysis is partial-equilibrium and does not capture citywide general-equilibrium adjustments. And while the permit event study cleanly identifies the supply channel, the home price effects are harder to separate from demand-side mechanisms.

Future work could extend this analysis in several directions. Quantifying the welfare costs of aldermanic discretion using a structural model of developer entry and household location choice would provide a more complete accounting than reduced-form estimates alone. Examining whether stringency affects the composition of housing supply, particularly the mix of affordable versus market-rate units, would speak directly to the equity concerns raised by HUD. And extending the empirical approach to other cities with ward-based governance or similar forms of local discretion could help assess whether the patterns documented here are specific to Chicago or reflect a more general phenomenon.
