\subsection{Why the Pooled RD Plots Slope}

The main-text RD plots slope upward toward the boundary on both sides. Table~\ref{tab:rd_distance_bins} shows that this is partly a composition fact of the sample: parcels closest to ward boundaries are more likely to be in mixed-use corridor zoning and, in the full new-construction sample, more likely to be multifamily. Figure~\ref{fig:rd_plot_style_comparison} compares the standard RD fits to level-only residual plots that remove fixed effects and controls without adding back running-distance slopes. The level-only plots are much flatter, confirming that the visual steepness in Figure~\ref{fig:rd_fe_plots} comes from the within-side RD approximation rather than from a large unconditional mean shift.

Table~\ref{tab:rd_slope_diagnostics} shows that replacing zoning-group fixed effects with exact zoning-code fixed effects attenuates both the jump and the slope terms, but the true-boundary discontinuity remains. By contrast, the placebo cutoff shifted 500 feet into the more stringent ward produces near-zero jumps for both FAR and DUPAC. Figure~\ref{fig:rd_slope_case_studies} maps the six steepest negative-slope ward pairs. These cases are concentrated in a small set of corridor-like environments rather than being representative of the full citywide boundary sample.

\subsection{Alternative Fixed Effects: Ward-Pair}

Table~\ref{tab:fe_pair_year} replaces segment fixed effects with ward-pair fixed effects, which pool across all segments of a given ward pair. This is a coarser comparison---ward-pair borders can stretch for miles through different neighborhoods---but it uses more variation. The estimates remain negative, but they are attenuated relative to the main segment FE estimates in Table~\ref{tab:segment_fe_500}.

\subsection{Donut RD Robustness}

One concern with spatial RD designs is that observations precisely at the boundary may be unusual---for example, if ward boundaries follow major streets that independently affect development. Figure~\ref{fig:rd_donut} implements a donut specification \citep{barreca_saving_2011, barreca_heapinginduced_2016} that excludes parcels within 25 or 50 feet of the boundary. In both donut plots, the fitted discontinuity remains negative, suggesting the main pattern is not driven only by parcels right at the cutoff.
