\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What the Paper Can and Cannot Say}

The strongest evidence in this paper concerns supply. Stricter aldermen reduce multifamily density near ward boundaries (Section~\ref{sec:empirical_results_density}), and when blocks are reassigned to stricter aldermen, the flow of high-discretion permits falls (Section~\ref{sec:empirical_results_permits}). These results are identified from two different sources of variation---cross-sectional comparisons at ward boundaries and dynamic responses to redistricting---and they tell the same story. Aldermanic discretion makes the development process slower, less predictable, and less productive for the types of projects that require meaningful review.

The price evidence is supportive but less definitive. Home prices rise roughly 2\% when blocks are assigned to stricter aldermen, consistent with supply restrictions getting capitalized into housing costs. But the price estimates are noisier, and I cannot fully distinguish supply-side mechanisms from demand-side stories in which stricter aldermen signal neighborhood stability.

It is useful to distinguish three margins along which aldermanic strictness affects housing supply. The first is the intensive margin: conditional on a developer seeking discretionary approval, the alderman affects the size and density of the approved project. This is the margin documented by \citet{khan_decentralized_2021}. The second is the extensive margin of realized development: developers in strict wards build smaller by-right projects that avoid discretionary review rather than proposing larger projects that would trigger it. My density estimates capture both of these margins. The third is the deterrence margin: projects that are never proposed because developers anticipate rejection or choose to invest elsewhere. This margin is unobserved by construction and means my estimates are best interpreted as lower bounds.

\subsection{Connection to the Broader Literature}

This paper adds to the evidence that land use regulations restrict housing supply and raise prices \citep{glaeser_why_2005, saiz_geographic_2010, turner_land_2014, duranton_urban_2023}. The contribution is to show that this mechanism operates at the hyper-local level, within a single city, driven by variation in informal discretionary power rather than differences in formal zoning codes. Ward-level variation in regulatory stringency creates meaningful supply distortions even within a unified municipal boundary.

The results also speak to the political economy of land use regulation \citep{fischel_homevoter_2009, hilber_origins_2013, ortalo-magne_political_2014}. Aldermanic privilege functions as a mechanism through which incumbent residents---particularly homeowners---can restrict development through political channels. The costs are borne by prospective residents, renters, and the city as a whole through a reduced tax base and diminished agglomeration economies. This is the same coordination failure documented across municipalities \citep{kulka_how_2022, monarrez_dividing_2023, bordeu_commuting_2025, mast_warding_2024}, operating within a single city through the fragmentation of informal authority.

\subsection{Policy Implications}

The results inform ongoing debates about reforming aldermanic privilege. The supply evidence suggests that the institution imposes real costs on housing production---not through a single dramatic veto, but through the cumulative effect of longer processing times, greater uncertainty, and the resulting deterrence of marginal projects.

Three policy approaches could address these costs. The first is centralization: shifting discretion from individual aldermen to citywide bodies that internalize the full benefits of housing production. The tradeoff is that centralization sacrifices local knowledge and may reduce democratic accountability. The second is expanding by-right development, so that projects complying with the zoning code proceed automatically without aldermanic review. This preserves community preferences as expressed in the code while eliminating the discretionary layer. The third is transparency: requiring aldermen to publicly state their positions on pending applications and creating systematic records of approval and rejection. Transparency could enable electoral accountability, though it may not change incentives if ward electorates are dominated by homeowners who benefit from restriction.

These approaches are not mutually exclusive. The key insight from this paper is that the status quo---decentralized discretion over development---generates measurable distortions to housing supply. Whether the benefits of local control outweigh those costs is a political judgment, but it should be informed by evidence on the magnitudes involved.
