\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Interpretation of Estimates}

The strongest evidence in this paper concerns the supply side of the housing market. More stringent aldermen reduce multifamily density near ward boundaries (Section~\ref{sec:empirical_results_density}), and when blocks are reassigned to more stringent aldermen through redistricting, the flow of high-discretion permits falls (Section~\ref{sec:empirical_results_permits}). These results come from two different sources of variation, cross-sectional comparisons at ward boundaries and dynamic responses to redistricting, and they tell the same story: aldermanic discretion makes the development process slower, less predictable, and less productive for the types of projects that require meaningful review. The price evidence is supportive but less definitive. Home prices move in the expected direction when blocks are assigned to more stringent aldermen, consistent with supply restrictions getting capitalized into housing costs, but the estimates are noisier and harder to separate from demand-side explanations.

It is useful to think about the margins of adjustment that these estimates capture. \citet{khan_decentralized_2021} documents the intensive margin: conditional on a developer seeking a rezoning, more stringent aldermen approve smaller projects. My density estimates capture this margin but also an extensive margin of realized development. Developers in more stringent wards appear to build smaller by-right projects that avoid discretionary review rather than proposing larger projects that would trigger it. The density effects I document therefore reflect both channels. What neither my estimates nor Khan's can capture is the deterrence margin: projects that are never proposed because developers anticipate rejection, face prohibitive uncertainty, or choose to invest in other jurisdictions entirely. This margin is unobserved by construction, which means the effects I estimate are best interpreted as lower bounds on the total supply impact of aldermanic discretion. The time-to-build framework in Section~\ref{sec:model} provides intuition for why deterrence may be quantitatively important: even a modest increase in expected regulatory delay can flip marginal projects from viable to unviable, and these projects leave no trace in the data.

Several mechanisms could contribute to this unmeasured deterrence. Regulatory uncertainty deters risk-averse developers even when the expected probability of approval is reasonable: the option value of waiting or building elsewhere may dominate proceeding with an uncertain process \citep{cunningham_house_2006, bloom_impact_2009}. The negotiation process itself imposes costs that are not reflected in final permit records, since aldermen may condition approval on design modifications, density reductions, or community benefit agreements. And developers report that navigating aldermanic approval is among the most uncertain and time-consuming aspects of Chicago development, leading some to focus investment in suburban jurisdictions with more predictable approval processes. This reallocation of development activity away from the city represents a real cost that does not appear in any permit database.

\subsection{Connection to the Broader Literature}

This paper adds to the evidence that land use regulations restrict housing supply and raise prices \citep{glaeser_why_2005, saiz_geographic_2010, turner_land_2014, duranton_urban_2023}. The contribution is to show that this mechanism operates at the hyper-local level within a single city, driven by variation in informal discretionary authority rather than differences in formal zoning codes. Ward-level differences in regulatory stringency create meaningful supply distortions even within a unified municipal boundary.

The results connect to the growing literature on regulatory process frictions and time-to-build as determinants of housing supply. \citet{paciorek_supply_2013} shows that regulation reduces supply primarily through its effect on new construction timelines, and \citet{soltas_how_2026} provides evidence that permitting costs are large enough to materially affect affordability. My results complement this work by providing causal evidence that variation in the discretionary layer of the regulatory process, not just the formal rules or average processing speed, generates economically meaningful supply differences. The permit event study is particularly informative here: it shows that the same physical block produces fewer high-discretion permits when its regulatory gatekeeper becomes more stringent, holding everything else about the location constant.

The results also speak to the political economy of land use regulation. \citet{fischel_homevoter_2009} argues that homeowners use local political institutions to restrict development and protect property values. Aldermanic privilege functions as exactly this kind of mechanism: incumbent residents, particularly homeowners, can use their alderman to restrict development through informal political channels, with costs borne by prospective residents, renters, and the city as a whole through a reduced tax base and diminished agglomeration economies \citep{ahlfeldt_economic_2019, duranton_economics_2020}. Prior work has documented how this coordination failure operates across municipalities \citep{hilber_origins_2013, ortalo-magne_political_2014, kulka_how_2022, monarrez_dividing_2023, mast_warding_2024, bordeu_commuting_2025}. My results show that a similar dynamic can operate within a single city when informal authority over development is decentralized across local representatives.

\subsection{Policy Implications}

The results inform ongoing debates about reforming aldermanic privilege in Chicago. The supply evidence suggests that the institution imposes real costs on housing production, not through a single dramatic veto but through the cumulative effect of longer processing times, greater uncertainty, and the resulting deterrence of marginal projects. As \citet{soltas_how_2026} emphasize more broadly, permitting frictions are a first-order determinant of housing affordability, and aldermanic privilege is a particularly stark example of how discretionary authority generates those frictions.

Several policy approaches could address these costs. Centralizing land use authority by shifting discretion to citywide bodies such as the Plan Commission or Zoning Board of Appeals would allow decision-makers to internalize the citywide benefits of housing production that individual aldermen rationally ignore. The tradeoff is that centralization sacrifices local knowledge and may reduce democratic accountability for land use decisions. Expanding by-right development, so that projects complying with the zoning code proceed automatically without aldermanic review, preserves community preferences as expressed in the code while eliminating the discretionary layer that generates uncertainty and delay. The limitation is reduced flexibility to address legitimate site-specific concerns not anticipated by general zoning categories. Greater transparency, requiring aldermen to publicly state positions on pending applications and creating systematic records of approval and rejection, could enable electoral accountability, though it may not change underlying incentives if ward electorates are dominated by homeowners who benefit from restriction.

An important caveat from the slides and from recent policy debates is that expanding by-right development alone may not be sufficient. Aldermen retain unilateral power to downzone parcels preemptively, which means they can effectively undo by-right protections before a developer even applies. Reforms that target only the permitting process without addressing the broader scope of aldermanic discretion over zoning may therefore have limited impact. The binding constraint is that aldermen interfere with both the zoning code and the permitting process, and effective reform likely needs to address both channels.

These approaches are not mutually exclusive, and the optimal policy likely involves some combination. The key insight from this paper is that the status quo of decentralized discretion over development generates measurable distortions to housing supply. Whether the benefits of local control outweigh those costs is ultimately a political judgment, but that judgment should be informed by evidence on the magnitudes involved.
