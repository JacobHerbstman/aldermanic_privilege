\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Interpretation of Estimates}

The strongest evidence in this paper concerns the supply side of the housing market. More stringent aldermen reduce multifamily density near ward boundaries (Section~\ref{sec:empirical_results_density}), and when blocks are reassigned to more stringent aldermen through redistricting, the flow of high-discretion permits falls (Section~\ref{sec:empirical_results_permits}). These results come from two different sources of variation and they tell the same story: aldermanic discretion makes the development process slower, less predictable, and less productive for the types of projects that require meaningful review. The price evidence is supportive but less definitive, with home prices moving in the expected direction but estimates that are noisier and harder to separate from demand-side explanations.

\citet{khan_decentralized_2021} documents the intensive margin of this institution: conditional on a developer seeking a rezoning, more stringent aldermen approve smaller projects. My density estimates capture this margin but also an extensive margin of realized development, where developers in more stringent wards build smaller by-right projects that avoid discretionary review rather than proposing larger projects that would trigger it. What neither my estimates nor Khan's can capture is the deterrence margin: projects that are never proposed because developers anticipate rejection or choose to invest elsewhere. The Fern Hill episode described in Section~\ref{sec:data} illustrates both the observed margins (the approved project was smaller than originally proposed) and the deterrence channel (four years of community meetings and aldermanic negotiation signals to other developers what attempting a large project entails). The time-to-build framework in Section~\ref{sec:model} provides intuition for why deterrence may be quantitatively important: even a modest increase in expected regulatory delay can flip marginal projects from viable to unviable, and these projects leave no trace in the data. My estimates are therefore best interpreted as lower bounds on the total supply impact of aldermanic discretion.

\subsection{Connection to the Broader Literature}

This paper contributes to the evidence that land use regulations restrict housing supply and raise prices \citep{glaeser_why_2005, saiz_geographic_2010, turner_land_2014, duranton_urban_2023}, showing that this mechanism operates at the hyper-local level within a single city through variation in informal discretionary authority rather than differences in formal zoning codes.

The results connect most directly to the literature on regulatory process frictions. \citet{paciorek_supply_2013} shows that regulation reduces supply primarily through its effect on new construction timelines, and \citet{soltas_how_2026} provides evidence that permitting costs materially affect affordability. My permit event study complements this work by showing that variation in the discretionary layer of the process, not just the formal rules or average processing speed, generates economically meaningful supply differences. The same physical block produces fewer high-discretion permits when its regulatory gatekeeper becomes more stringent, holding everything else about the location constant.

The political economy channel is consistent with the homevoter hypothesis of \citet{fischel_homevoter_2009}: aldermanic privilege gives incumbent residents, particularly homeowners, a mechanism to restrict development through informal political channels, with costs borne by prospective residents, renters, and the city through a reduced tax base and diminished agglomeration economies \citep{ahlfeldt_economic_2019}. Prior work has documented this coordination failure across municipalities \citep{hilber_origins_2013, kulka_how_2022, monarrez_dividing_2023, mast_warding_2024, bordeu_commuting_2025}. My results show it operating within a single city through fragmented informal authority.

\subsection{Policy Implications}

The results inform ongoing debates about reforming aldermanic privilege. The supply effects I document accumulate through longer processing times, greater uncertainty, and the resulting deterrence of marginal projects, rather than through any single dramatic veto.

Centralizing land use authority, by shifting discretion from individual aldermen to citywide bodies, would allow decision-makers to internalize the full benefits of housing production at the cost of sacrificing local knowledge. Expanding by-right development, so that code-compliant projects proceed automatically without aldermanic review, would eliminate the discretionary layer while preserving community preferences as expressed in the zoning code. But as documented in Section~\ref{sec:data}, aldermen retain unilateral power to downzone parcels preemptively, which means they can effectively undo by-right protections before a developer applies. The Reilly and Solis downzoning episodes are illustrative: reforms targeting only the permitting process without constraining aldermanic control over the zoning code itself may have limited impact. Effective reform likely needs to address both channels.
