\begin{abstract}
Land use regulation constrains housing supply, but formal zoning rules are only part of the regulatory environment developers face.
I study informal regulatory discretion through Chicago's ``aldermanic privilege,'' under which city council members can effectively block, delay, or reshape development in their wards.
I construct alderman-level stringency scores from permit processing times and use complementary ward-boundary regression discontinuity and event-study approaches to compare places exposed to aldermen with different levels of regulatory stringency.
At ward borders, a one standard deviation increase in aldermanic stringency lowers multifamily construction density by roughly 12-14\%.
Using an event-study approach exploiting ward redistricting in 2015, I find that census blocks reassigned to more stringent aldermen experience a 7.4\% decline in permits.
Spatial regression discontinuity estimates provide supporting evidence of higher listed rents and higher home sale prices on the more stringent side of ward boundaries.
Together, the results show that informal regulatory discretion can reduce housing supply and harm affordability, calling into question the assumption that zoning reform alone will necessarily lead to more housing construction.
\end{abstract}
