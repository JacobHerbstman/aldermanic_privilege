\begin{abstract}
Land use regulation constrains housing supply, but most evidence focuses on formal rules such as zoning codes. I study the role of informal regulatory discretion through Chicago's ``aldermanic privilege,'' under which city council members can effectively block or approve development in their wards. Using permit processing times to construct alderman-level stringency scores, I document large differences in the local regulatory environment that are not explained by geography or demographics. A border discontinuity design shows that new multifamily construction is roughly 15\% less dense on the more stringent side of ward boundaries. An event study around ward redistricting shows that blocks reassigned to more stringent aldermen experience a 9\% decline in high-discretion permits over the following five years. I also find suggestive evidence that these supply restrictions pass through into higher home prices. Taken together, the results imply that informal discretionary authority materially shapes housing supply and that reforms to local land use decision-making could affect housing affordability.
\end{abstract}
