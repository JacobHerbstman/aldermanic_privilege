\begin{abstract}
Local land use regulation restricts housing supply and raises prices, but most evidence comes from variation across municipalities. I study how the fragmentation of land use authority within a city affects the development process, focusing on Chicago's ``aldermanic privilege''---the informal institution granting each of 50 aldermen effective veto power over development in their ward. Using permit processing times for high-discretion permits, I construct aldermanic strictness scores that capture the bureaucratic frictions each alderman imposes on development. Two empirical designs establish that these frictions reduce housing supply. First, a spatial discontinuity design shows that new multifamily construction is 10--16\% less dense on the stricter side of ward boundaries. Second, when ward redistricting reassigns blocks to stricter aldermen, the flow of high-discretion permits falls by approximately 9\%. Home sale prices rise roughly 2\% following reassignment to stricter aldermen, consistent with supply restrictions being capitalized into housing costs. These findings demonstrate that within-city fragmentation of land use authority generates coordination failures analogous to those documented across municipalities: individual aldermen rationally restrict development, but the collective outcome is undersupply of housing.
\end{abstract}
