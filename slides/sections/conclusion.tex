\begin{frame}
    \frametitle{Discussion \& Policy Implications}
    
    \textbf{Why is this hard to reform?}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Expanding by-right development is not enough}: Aldermen retain unilateral power to downzone parcels preemptively 
        \item \textbf{Centralizing authority has real costs}: Aldermen do have local information that a citywide body would lack, but they also have incentives to use their power to extract rents from developers and protect incumbent constituents
    \end{itemize}

    \vspace{0.5em}
    \textbf{The binding constraint}: Aldermen interfere with zoning \textit{and} permitting processes
    \begin{itemize}
        \item My results capture frictions in the \textit{permitting} process, but aldermen also control the zoning code in their ward
        \item Reforms need to target broad discretionary power, not just by-right zoning reforms
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
    \frametitle{Conclusion}
    
    \textbf{Summary of findings}:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Substantial variation in regulatory stringency across aldermen which leads to large differences in the regulatory environment for housing development across wards
        \item More stringent aldermen $\Rightarrow$ lower multifamily density at ward borders by $\approx 10\%$
        \item Leads to $3-4\%$ higher rents, which appears to be driven by reduced supply
    \end{itemize}

    \textbf{Implications}:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Regulatory discretion, which manifests in discrete changes in regulatory stringency across the city, lowers density + housing supply and raises rents
        \item Within-city variation in the regulatory environment matters, not just cross-municipality differences
        \item Reforms should target decision-making authority and discretion, not just by-right zoning reforms
    \end{itemize}

\end{frame}
