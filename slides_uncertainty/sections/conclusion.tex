\begin{frame}
    \frametitle{Discussion \& Policy Implications}
    
    \textbf{Core problem}: Regulatory uncertainty and permitting frictions raise the cost of housing development
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Longer permitting times $\rightarrow$ higher carrying costs for developers
        \item Fewer projects initiated $\rightarrow$ constrained rental supply $\rightarrow$ higher rents
        \item Effects concentrated in the rental market, not capitalized into sale prices
    \end{itemize}

    \vspace{0.5em}
    \textbf{Policy alternatives to reduce regulatory friction}:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Streamline permitting}: Reduce discretion and processing times to lower uncertainty for developers
        \item \textbf{Transfer decision-making authority}: Shift zoning decisions to the citywide level (i.e.\ mayor, zoning board)
        \item \textbf{Citywide preemption}: Override local vetoes for certain housing types \\
              {\small (e.g., CA SB-35, MA Ch. 40B, federal H.O.M.E. Act proposals)}
    \end{enumerate}
\end{frame}

\begin{frame}
    \frametitle{Conclusion}
    
    \textbf{Summary of findings}:
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Aldermen vary substantially in regulatory uncertainty (permit processing times)
        \item More uncertain aldermen $\Rightarrow$ lower multifamily FAR and DUPAC at borders (about 7--10\% in border FE)
        \item $\sim$1.5-3\% higher rents which appears to be driven by supply
    \end{enumerate}

    \vspace{0.5em}
    \textbf{Implications}:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Regulatory uncertainty in decentralized zoning lowers supply and raises rents
        \item Within-city fragmentation matters, not just across municipalities
        \item Reforms should target permitting frictions and reduce aldermanic discretion
    \end{itemize}

    \textbf{Future work}:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Model developer location choice decision to run counterfactuals on policy reforms
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}
